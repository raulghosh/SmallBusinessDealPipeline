# Financing Structures Reference

## Overview

Four primary structures are available for acquiring a service business. The buyer's H1B status
does not restrict which structures are available — it adds the GM salary expense that must be
modeled in all DSCR calculations.

**Decision priority:** Always start with SBA 7(a) primary. Only deviate if DSCR fails or deal
size falls outside SBA optimal range. Seller financing is almost always additive, not a replacement.

---

## Structure 1: SBA 7(a) Primary

**Best for:** Deals with $750K-$4M asking price where SDE supports DSCR ≥1.25x after GM
salary adjustment.

### How It Works
- Buyer provides equity injection (minimum 10% of asking price for acquisitions)
- SBA-approved lender provides the remaining 90% of purchase price
- SBA guarantees 75-85% of the loan to the lender
- Lender holds first lien position on all business assets

### Key Terms
- Maximum loan: $5M (SBA program cap)
- Term: 10 years for business acquisition; up to 25 years if real estate is included
- Rate: Variable, Prime + 2.75% (~10.5% in 2025-2026)
- Guarantee fee: 0.25%-3.75% of guaranteed portion (varies by loan size)
- Prepayment penalty: Yes, for loans 15+ years; none for 10-year loans
- Equity injection: Minimum 10% for acquisitions of established businesses

### Pros
- Lowest down payment structure available (10% vs 20-30% conventional)
- Long amortization period keeps monthly payments manageable
- Competitive rates for well-qualified borrowers
- SBA guarantee enables deals conventional lenders won't touch
- Once approved, relatively standard process across lenders

### Cons
- Heavy documentation requirement (3 years business + personal tax returns, plus more)
- Timeline: 60-120 days from application to close
- Personal guarantee required — buyer's personal assets are at risk
- Lender has approval authority, not SBA — lenders can decline even SBA-eligible deals
- Environmental assessment required for car wash (cost and time)
- Business valuation required for deals over $250K (adds cost)

### H1B Considerations
- Passive investor structure must be explicitly documented
- GM must be identified, hired (or offer extended), and employed at closing
- Immigration attorney letter required in loan package
- H1B buyer's personal guarantee is standard; consult immigration attorney to confirm compliance

### Worked Example — $1.5M Asking Price, $300K SDE

| Item | Calculation | Result |
|------|------------|--------|
| Asking price | — | $1,500,000 |
| Equity injection (10%) | $1,500,000 × 10% | $150,000 |
| SBA loan amount | $1,500,000 × 90% | $1,350,000 |
| Monthly payment (10-yr, 10.5%) | $1,350,000 × 0.01348 | $18,198/mo |
| Annual debt service | $18,198 × 12 | $218,376 |
| GM salary (revenue ~$1.5M est.) | Revenue bracket lookup | $75,000 |
| Adjusted NOI | $300,000 - $75,000 | $225,000 |
| DSCR | $225,000 / $218,376 | 1.03x — MARGINAL |
| Cash-on-cash (if approved) | ($225K - $218,376) / $150K | 4.4% — POOR |

**Verdict on this example at SBA Primary only:** DSCR is below 1.25x. This deal needs seller
financing or a price reduction to become financeable at SBA terms. See Structure 2 (Hybrid).

## Structure 2: Seller Financing (Supplement or Standalone)

**Best for:** When SDE produces borderline DSCR at SBA-only terms, or when seller is motivated
to close quickly. Almost always used in combination with SBA (hybrid structure).

### How It Works
- Seller "carries back" a portion of the purchase price as a loan to the buyer
- Buyer pays seller over 3-7 years, typically at 5-7% interest
- Seller note is subordinated to the SBA lender (SBA takes first position)
- Reduces SBA loan amount, which reduces monthly debt service, which improves DSCR

### Typical Terms
- Amount: 10-20% of purchase price (SBA generally allows seller notes up to 20%)
- Interest rate: 5-7% (negotiable)
- Term: 3-7 years
- Balloon payment at end: common
- Standby provision: SBA may require seller note payments to "stand by" (pause) for the first
  24 months of the loan — confirm with lender

### What Seller Financing Signals
A seller who offers seller financing is making the most credible signal of confidence available:
they are putting their own money on the line that the business will continue to perform after
sale. Treat unprompted seller financing as a genuine green flag.

A seller who refuses all seller financing — especially on a marginal-DSCR deal — warrants
hard scrutiny. They may be unwilling to share in the risk because they know the business
doesn't support the asking price.

### Pros
- Fills the equity gap (reduces buyer's cash out-of-pocket)
- Improves DSCR by reducing SBA loan size
- Signals seller confidence
- Faster to close than conventional financing alternative
- Negotiating tool: offering to accept seller financing at a higher price can work for motivated sellers

### Cons
- Seller note comes with representations and warranties
- Non-compete from seller is usually tied to the note (good for buyer)
- Standby provision may mean no seller note payments in first 2 years (helps DSCR, but seller may dislike it)
- Subordinated position means seller is unsecured if business fails

### H1B Considerations
No additional H1B-specific concerns beyond the standard GM requirement. Seller financing
structures are neutral with respect to visa status.

## Structure 3: Hybrid SBA + Seller Note (Recommended Default)

**This is the most commonly recommended structure for this buyer's target deal range.**

### How It Works
- SBA 7(a) loan: 75-80% of purchase price
- Seller note: 10-15% of purchase price (subordinated to SBA)
- Buyer equity injection: 10% of purchase price
- Total: 100% of purchase price covered

### Why This Is the Recommended Default
- Minimizes buyer's cash out-of-pocket (only 10% equity injection)
- Reduces SBA loan amount by 10-15%, significantly improving DSCR
- Keeps total financing achievable even for deals where SBA-only DSCR is marginal (1.0-1.2x)
- Standard structure that SBA lenders understand and accept

### Worked Example — $1.5M Asking Price, $300K SDE (Same Deal as Above)

| Item | Calculation | Result |
|------|------------|--------|
| Asking price | — | $1,500,000 |
| Buyer equity injection (10%) | $1,500,000 × 10% | $150,000 |
| Seller note (15%) | $1,500,000 × 15% | $225,000 |
| SBA loan (75%) | $1,500,000 × 75% | $1,125,000 |
| SBA monthly payment (10-yr, 10.5%) | $1,125,000 × 0.01348 | $15,165/mo |
| SBA annual debt service | $15,165 × 12 | $181,980 |
| Seller note payment (6%, 5-yr) | $225,000 amortized | ~$4,350/mo |
| Seller note annual payment | $4,350 × 12 | $52,200 |
| Total annual debt service | $181,980 + $52,200 | $234,180 |
| GM salary (revenue ~$1.5M est.) | — | $75,000 |
| Adjusted NOI | $300,000 - $75,000 | $225,000 |
| DSCR | $225,000 / $234,180 | 0.96x — FAIL |

**Note:** Even with seller carry, this particular deal ($1.5M asking, $300K SDE) doesn't clear
1.25x DSCR at hybrid structure. The deal needs a price reduction OR the SDE needs to be higher
than stated. This is why the reverse DSCR calculation is important (see `dscr_calculator.md`).

### Modified Hybrid Example — Better Price Point ($1.1M Asking, $300K SDE)

| Item | Calculation | Result |
|------|------------|--------|
| Asking price | — | $1,100,000 |
| Buyer equity (10%) | — | $110,000 |
| Seller note (15%) | — | $165,000 |
| SBA loan (75%) | — | $825,000 |
| SBA monthly payment | $825,000 × 0.01348 | $11,121/mo |
| SBA annual debt service | $11,121 × 12 | $133,452 |
| Seller note payment (6%, 5-yr) | ~$3,190/mo | $38,280/yr |
| Total annual debt service | — | $171,732 |
| GM salary | — | $75,000 |
| Adjusted NOI | $300K - $75K | $225,000 |
| DSCR | $225,000 / $171,732 | 1.31x — PASS |
| Cash-on-cash | ($225K - $171,732) / $110K | 48.4% — EXCELLENT |

**At $1.1M, the same deal becomes strongly financeable.** This is the value of running the
reverse DSCR calculation: it tells you the max price that makes the deal work.

## Structure 4: Conventional Small Business Loan

**Best for:** Very clean deals where buyer wants faster close or is in a competitive bidding
situation, OR for smaller deals ($300K-$600K) where SBA overhead isn't justified.

### Key Terms
- Down payment: 20-30% (significantly more than SBA)
- Term: 5-7 years (shorter than SBA, higher monthly payments)
- Rate: 11-14% (higher than SBA)
- Documentation: Less than SBA, but still substantial
- Timeline: 30-60 days (faster than SBA)

### When Conventional Beats SBA
- Buyer is in a competitive situation and timeline matters
- Deal is smaller ($400K-$600K asking) and SBA overhead (time, cost) isn't justified
- Buyer wants to avoid SBA's 10-year tie-up period and prepayment restrictions
- Lender relationship exists that makes conventional faster and easier

### H1B Considerations
Same active-management requirement applies. SBA's rules reflect regulatory requirements that
conventional lenders often mirror in their own underwriting.

### Worked Example — $1.5M Asking Price, $300K SDE

| Item | Calculation | Result |
|------|------------|--------|
| Asking price | — | $1,500,000 |
| Down payment (25%) | — | $375,000 |
| Loan amount | — | $1,125,000 |
| Monthly payment (7-yr, 12%) | $1,125,000 × 0.01749 | $19,676/mo |
| Annual debt service | $19,676 × 12 | $236,112 |
| GM salary | — | $75,000 |
| Adjusted NOI | $300,000 - $75,000 | $225,000 |
| DSCR | $225,000 / $236,112 | 0.95x — FAIL |

**At the same deal terms, conventional is worse than SBA for DSCR** because the shorter term
creates higher monthly payments. Conventional only wins on speed, not on financing terms.

## Structure 5: Cash Acquisition

**Best for:** Smaller deals ($300K-$600K asking price range) where buyer has sufficient
liquidity and wants maximum negotiating leverage and fastest close.

### Pros
- Fastest close (30-45 days vs 60-120 days for SBA)
- No lender requirements, no documentation burden
- Maximum negotiating leverage — sellers strongly prefer cash buyers
- No debt service — all cash flow goes directly to investor
- No risk of loan falling through in underwriting

### Cons
- Capital concentration risk — significant liquidity tied up in one asset
- No leverage on returns — cash-on-cash return equals SDE yield directly
- For a $700K deal with $140K SDE after GM: 14% yield, decent but unlevered
- Opportunity cost — same capital in SBA deal could control 10x the asset value

### When to Model Cash Acquisition
- User signals they have sufficient liquidity and want to avoid SBA process
- Asking price is in the $300K-$600K range where SBA overhead is disproportionate
- Deal is competitive and cash offer is the only way to win

### Worked Example — $650K Asking, $175K SDE (Laundromat)

| Item | Calculation | Result |
|------|------------|--------|
| Asking price | — | $650,000 |
| Cash required | — | $650,000 |
| Debt service | — | $0 |
| GM salary (PT, laundromat) | — | $50,000 |
| Net cash flow to investor | $175K - $50K | $125,000/yr |
| Cash-on-cash return | $125,000 / $650,000 | 19.2% |

**19.2% unlevered return is excellent.** Compare to the SBA-financed version of the same
deal (shown in `dscr_calculator.md` Example 1): 46% cash-on-cash — but that requires only
$65K of the buyer's capital instead of $650K.

**The SBA version produces a much higher percentage return on deployed capital**,
which is why SBA financing is almost always superior when DSCR is achievable.

## Structure Comparison Summary

For a $1.5M asking price deal with $300K SDE and $75K GM salary (Adjusted NOI $225K):

| Structure | Equity In | Loan Amount | Monthly DS | Annual DS | DSCR | C-o-C |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|-----------|-----------|------|-------|
| SBA Primary (10%) | $150K | $1,350K | $18,198 | $218,376 | 1.03x FAIL | 4.4% |
| SBA + Seller Note (hybrid) | $150K | $1,125K SBA | $19,515 | $234,180 | 0.96x FAIL | neg |
| Conventional (25%) | $375K | $1,125K | $19,676 | $236,112 | 0.95x FAIL | neg |
| Cash | $1,500K | $0 | $0 | $0 | n/a | 15.0% |

**At $1.5M asking with $300K SDE, this deal does not pencil on a leveraged basis.**
The maximum financeable price for this deal is approximately $1.1M (see `dscr_calculator.md`
for the reverse DSCR calculation that produces this number).

## Choosing the Right Structure — Decision Logic

```
DSCR at SBA Primary ≥ 1.35x?
  → YES: Recommend SBA Primary. No seller note needed.

DSCR at SBA Primary 1.10-1.34x?
  → YES: Recommend Hybrid (SBA + Seller Note). Request 10-15% seller carry in offer.

DSCR at SBA Primary < 1.10x?
  → Run hybrid scenario.
  → Hybrid DSCR ≥ 1.25x? → Hybrid with larger seller carry (20%), proceed with conditions.
  → Hybrid DSCR < 1.25x? → Calculate max financeable price. Deal needs renegotiation.

Deal asking price < $600K AND buyer has liquidity?
  → Present cash acquisition as alternative. Show yield comparison.

Buyer signals timeline urgency (deal is competitive)?
  → Present conventional as alternative. Quantify speed advantage vs. higher down payment.
```
