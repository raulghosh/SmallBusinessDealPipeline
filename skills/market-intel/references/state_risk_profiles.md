# State Risk Profiles — Market Intel Reference

Covers the six target states: GA, FL, AL, TN, NC, SC.
Use the relevant section when running market intel for a deal in that state.
This reference reflects the regulatory and market environment as of 2025-2026.
Always note that conditions evolve — buyer's counsel should verify before close.

---

## How to Read This Document

Each state profile covers:
1. **Business-Friendliness Rating** — overall risk for owning a service business (LOW / MEDIUM / HIGH risk)
2. **Minimum Wage Trajectory** — current rate and scheduled increases (critical for janitorial labor costs)
3. **Vertical-Specific Regulations** — what applies to janitorial, car wash, and laundromat operations
4. **Environmental Regulations** — water recycling, chemical disposal, waste handling
5. **Employment Law** — at-will status, workers comp, non-compete enforceability
6. **Metro Market Notes** — key metros and their local competitive environment
7. **Insurance Environment** — state-specific workers comp and GL context

## GEORGIA (GA)

### Business-Friendliness: LOW Risk (Favorable)

Georgia is consistently rated one of the top 5 most business-friendly states in the US
(Site Selection magazine, CNBC rankings). Atlanta is a major growth market. The state
actively courts small business investment and has limited regulatory overreach for
service-sector businesses.

### Minimum Wage Trajectory
- **Current rate:** $7.25/hr (follows federal minimum; no state increase scheduled as of 2026)
- **Trajectory:** STABLE — no active legislation to raise the state minimum wage
- **Metro note:** Atlanta employers often pay above minimum informally due to labor market
  competition, but there is no legal mandate above $7.25. Effective minimum for competitive
  janitorial hiring in Atlanta metro is $14-16/hr due to labor market.
- **Impact on janitorial:** Labor cost pressure is market-driven, not legislatively driven.
  Model wages at $14-16/hr for Atlanta placements; $12-14/hr for secondary metros.

### Vertical-Specific Regulations

**Janitorial/Commercial Cleaning:**
- No state-level janitorial licensing requirement (beyond general business registration)
- Bonding required by most commercial clients as a contract term (not a state requirement)
- No mandated green cleaning requirements, though commercial clients (especially healthcare)
  increasingly specify EPA Safer Choice products
- Background check requirements are client-driven, not state-mandated

**Car Wash:**
- Georgia EPD (Environmental Protection Division) regulates stormwater discharge
- Car wash operators must comply with NPDES (National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System)
  stormwater permit requirements if discharging to surface water
- Water recycling systems are not currently mandated statewide, but municipalities in metro
  Atlanta are increasingly encouraging them
- Chemical storage: OSHA Hazard Communication standards apply (federal)
- No specific car wash industry licensing beyond standard business registration

**Laundromat:**
- No state-specific laundromat licensing
- Commercial washing equipment must comply with standard electrical and plumbing codes
- Energy efficiency: Georgia does not mandate specific equipment efficiency standards beyond
  federal Energy Star requirements
- Coin-operated amusement and vending machine tax rules may apply — verify with tax counsel

### Environmental Regulations
- Georgia EPD oversees water quality and hazardous waste disposal
- Car wash wastewater: must be handled per EPD stormwater rules; direct discharge to storm
  drains without treatment is prohibited in most metro jurisdictions
- Chemical disposal (solvents, cleaning agents): subject to RCRA hazardous waste rules
- **Risk level: LOW-MEDIUM** — regulations exist but are manageable and well-understood by
  industry operators

### Employment Law
- **At-will employment:** YES — strong at-will state; easy to hire and separate
- **Non-compete enforceability:** MODERATE — Georgia reformed its non-compete law in 2011
  (Georgia Restrictive Covenants Act). Reasonable non-competes (geographic scope, time
  limits) are enforceable. Janitorial operators can protect client lists.
- **Workers comp:** Required for employers with 3+ employees. Class code 9014 (janitorial)
  rates typically 5-7% of payroll in Georgia. Car wash and laundromat rates lower (2-4%).
- **Wage theft / overtime:** Standard FLSA applies; no state supplement
- **Contractor misclassification:** Georgia follows federal IRS standards; scrutiny is
  moderate. Janitorial firms relying heavily on 1099 workers face some IRS/DOL risk.

### Metro Market Notes

**Atlanta (Fulton, DeKalb, Gwinnett, Cobb, Clayton counties):**
- Largest metro in the Southeast; extremely active commercial cleaning market
- Commercial cleaning: 200+ independent operators competing in metro; national brands
  (Coverall, Jan-Pro, ServiceMaster) heavily franchised and active
- Car wash: significant express tunnel saturation in suburban corridors (Kennesaw,
  Marietta, Alpharetta, Roswell, Duluth have 3-5+ tunnels each)
- Laundromat: strong demand in urban core (Decatur, East Atlanta, College Park, Forest
  Park) due to high renter density; gentrification is active in some pockets

**Savannah:**
- Smaller market; lower competitive density across all three verticals
- Tourism economy creates commercial cleaning demand for hotels/hospitality
- Car wash market is underserved relative to population growth (port expansion driving growth)

**Augusta / Columbus / Macon:**
- Secondary markets; lower competitive density; less PE activity
- Laundromat opportunities exist in working-class neighborhoods with stable renter bases
- Commercial cleaning market is less mature than Atlanta; national chains less dominant

### Insurance Environment
- **Workers comp:** MODERATE premium environment; competitive private market
- **General liability:** Competitive market; standard rates apply
- **Hurricane/wind:** Less of a concern than FL; northern GA has minimal storm risk
- **Overall insurance cost pressure: LOW-MEDIUM**

## FLORIDA (FL)

### Business-Friendliness: MEDIUM Risk (Mixed)

Florida has no state income tax and is business-friendly in many respects, but presents
specific elevated risks for service businesses: hurricane property damage, workers comp
costs (among the highest in the nation for certain classes), and a highly competitive
labor market in South Florida. The regulatory environment for environmental compliance
(EPD/DEP) is stricter than most Southeast states.

### Minimum Wage Trajectory
- **Current rate:** $13.00/hr (2025); scheduled increases to $14.00/hr in Sept 2026 and
  $15.00/hr by Sept 2027
- **Trajectory:** RISING — Florida voters passed Amendment 2 in 2020 mandating $15/hr
  by 2026. After $15, future increases are tied to CPI.
- **Impact on janitorial:** SIGNIFICANT. Florida janitorial businesses are facing a
  legislatively mandated 15-20% labor cost increase over 2025-2027. Any deal analysis
  must model wages at $14-15/hr immediately, not at current floor.
- **Mitigation:** Pass-throughs on commercial contracts may be possible for operators
  with strong client relationships, but price-sensitive customers may push back.

### Vertical-Specific Regulations

**Janitorial/Commercial Cleaning:**
- No state janitorial license required, but some counties (Miami-Dade, Broward) have
  additional business certification requirements
- Workers comp is compulsory for all employers with 1+ employees in construction;
  4+ employees in non-construction industries — verify exact threshold with FL DOL
- Background checks: client-driven (especially for healthcare/hospitality)
- Bonding: typically required by commercial clients as a contract term

**Car Wash:**
- Florida DEP (Department of Environmental Protection) regulates wastewater discharge;
  car wash effluent regulations are among the stricter in the Southeast
- Water recycling systems are strongly encouraged and, in some counties, required for
  new operations. Retrofitting existing operations can be costly.
- Environmental compliance permits may be required (NPDES stormwater permit, industrial
  wastewater permit if discharging to sewer)
- Hurricane preparedness: car wash equipment, conveyor systems, and roofing are all
  hurricane-exposure assets. Insurance premiums reflect this.

**Laundromat:**
- Standard business registration; no state-specific laundromat licensing
- Utility costs are a significant factor in Florida — commercial electric and water rates
  are generally higher than interior Southeast states
- Energy efficiency standards for commercial washers/dryers follow federal requirements

### Environmental Regulations
- **Florida DEP is one of the most active state environmental agencies in the Southeast.**
- Car wash wastewater: strict — zero discharge to stormwater drains is effectively required
  in most metro areas; treatment or closed-loop recycling is standard expectation
- Hazardous materials storage: DEP oversees storage tank regulations (underground storage
  tanks relevant for some car wash chemical systems)
- **Risk level: MEDIUM-HIGH** for car wash; LOW for janitorial and laundromat

### Employment Law
- **At-will employment:** YES — Florida is a strong at-will state
- **Non-compete enforceability:** HIGH — Florida has one of the most employer-friendly
  non-compete statutes in the US (F.S. 542.335). Courts routinely enforce them. Buyers
  should ensure seller signs a robust non-compete as part of asset purchase agreement.
- **Workers comp:** Required for all employers (4+ employees non-construction, 1+ construction).
  CRITICAL: Florida workers comp rates are elevated for service businesses.
  - Janitorial (class 9014): 6-9% of payroll in Florida — meaningfully higher than GA/TN
  - Car wash: 3-5% of payroll
  - This is a direct impact on SDE for any Florida janitorial business; verify actual
    workers comp spend during diligence
- **Contractor misclassification:** Florida has been active in enforcement; janitorial
  operators using 1099 workers extensively should be scrutinized

### Metro Market Notes

**Jacksonville (Duval County + surrounding):**
- Large market; significant commercial real estate sector creates cleaning demand
- Car wash: growing market; multiple express tunnels have opened 2020-2025 but less
  saturated than Tampa/Orlando
- Hurricane risk: northeastern Florida, lower than South FL but still notable
- Labor market: competitive; effective wages for janitorial are $13-15/hr

**Orlando / Tampa (not primary targets but relevant context):**
- Highly competitive; PE-backed janitorial operations present
- Car wash: extremely saturated in suburban Tampa (New Tampa, Wesley Chapel, Brandon
  corridors have 5+ express tunnels each in some areas)

**Pensacola / Tallahassee:**
- Secondary markets; lower competitive density
- Closer in character to Alabama/Georgia secondary markets

### Insurance Environment
- **CRITICAL RISK: Hurricane / wind / flood coverage**
  - Since 2022, Florida property insurance market has experienced market exits, premium
    spikes (30-60% increases), and coverage availability problems
  - Car wash operators: property coverage (for equipment and structure) is expensive and
    in some cases hard to place without surplus lines market
  - Laundromats: building contents coverage impacted by same market
  - Any Florida deal MUST include a specific line item for verified current insurance costs —
    do not use 2020-2022 figures as benchmarks
- **Workers comp:** High premium environment; factor into SDE verification
- **General liability:** Competitive; standard market available
- **Overall insurance cost pressure: HIGH (property/wind) + HIGH (workers comp for janitorial)**

## ALABAMA (AL)

### Business-Friendliness: LOW Risk (Very Favorable)

Alabama is one of the most business-friendly states in the South. Low regulatory burden,
low cost of living, and minimal state government interference in small business operations.
Workers comp costs are below Southeast average. No major pending legislation affecting
target verticals.

### Minimum Wage Trajectory
- **Current rate:** $7.25/hr (federal minimum; Alabama has no state minimum wage law)
- **Trajectory:** VERY STABLE — Alabama has actively resisted minimum wage increases.
  No scheduled increases. Very low probability of change in near term.
- **Impact on janitorial:** Favorable. Birmingham and Huntsville effective wages for
  janitorial are $11-13/hr (market-driven). Montgomery/Tuscaloosa/Mobile: $10-12/hr.
  Labor cost structure is favorable relative to FL, NC, and TN.

### Vertical-Specific Regulations

**Janitorial/Commercial Cleaning:**
- No state janitorial licensing requirement
- Standard business registration required
- Workers comp: required for employers with 5+ employees
- Environmental: minimal regulatory burden for cleaning chemicals (federal OSHA/EPA apply)

**Car Wash:**
- ADEM (Alabama Department of Environmental Management) regulates wastewater discharge
- Regulations are less stringent than FL/NC; car wash wastewater management is manageable
- Water recycling: not currently mandated statewide; individual municipalities may have
  requirements in their commercial zoning
- Standard environmental compliance costs are lower than Florida

**Laundromat:**
- No state-specific licensing; standard business registration
- Utility costs: lower than FL; natural gas and electric rates are favorable
- Equipment efficiency: follow federal standards

### Environmental Regulations
- ADEM is less aggressive than FL DEP or NC DEQ
- Car wash: wastewater management required but not onerous relative to other states
- Hazardous waste: RCRA applies (federal); state enforcement is moderate
- **Risk level: LOW** for all three verticals

### Employment Law
- **At-will employment:** YES — strong at-will state
- **Non-compete enforceability:** HIGH — Alabama courts enforce reasonable non-competes
- **Workers comp:** Required for 5+ employees. Rates below Southeast average.
  - Janitorial (class 9014): 4-6% of payroll — favorable
  - Car wash: 2-3% of payroll
- **Contractor classification:** Standard federal rules; Alabama enforcement is moderate

### Metro Market Notes

**Birmingham (Jefferson, Shelby, Blount counties):**
- Second-largest Alabama metro; stable industrial/commercial economy
- Janitorial: moderate competitive density; national chains less dominant than Atlanta
- Car wash: growing market; suburban Hoover/Vestavia Hills have express tunnel growth
  but not yet saturated
- Laundromat: strong demand in working-class neighborhoods; Northport/Tuscaloosa is
  a separate but adjacent market
- University of Alabama proximity creates steady rental population in Tuscaloosa

**Huntsville:**
- Fast-growing tech/defense corridor; highest household income in Alabama
- Commercial cleaning demand from defense contractors and tech facilities
- Car wash: newer market; growth trajectory favorable

**Mobile:**
- Gulf Coast exposure (hurricane risk lower than FL coast but present)
- Moderate market size; less competitive density in all three verticals

### Insurance Environment
- **Workers comp:** Among the lowest rates in the Southeast — favorable
- **General liability:** Competitive market; standard rates
- **Property:** Lower hurricane risk than FL coast; wind coverage more affordable
- **Overall insurance cost pressure: LOW**

## TENNESSEE (TN)

### Business-Friendliness: LOW Risk (Very Favorable)

Tennessee ranks consistently in the top 5 nationally for business climate. No state income
tax on wages (as of 2022, Tennessee fully eliminated its Hall income tax on investment
income). Pro-business legislature, low regulatory burden, strong population growth in
Nashville corridor. One of the best states in the target geography for business acquisition.

### Minimum Wage Trajectory
- **Current rate:** $7.25/hr (federal minimum; Tennessee has no state minimum wage law)
- **Trajectory:** VERY STABLE — no active legislation; strong political resistance to
  minimum wage increases
- **Impact on janitorial:** Favorable. Nashville effective wages for competitive janitorial
  hiring: $13-15/hr (market pressure, not legal mandate). Knoxville/Chattanooga: $11-13/hr.
  Memphis: $11-13/hr.
- **Note:** Nashville's rapid growth has created significant labor competition — wages are
  rising faster than in most TN metros due to construction and hospitality sector competition
  for workers.

### Vertical-Specific Regulations

**Janitorial/Commercial Cleaning:**
- No state janitorial licensing requirement
- Standard business registration
- Workers comp: required for employers with 5+ employees (some exceptions for sole
  proprietors)
- Background checks: client-driven; healthcare facilities in particular require them

**Car Wash:**
- TDEC (Tennessee Department of Environment and Conservation) regulates water discharge
- Stormwater permits required if discharging to surface waters
- Generally manageable compliance environment relative to FL/NC
- Nashville metro has implemented water conservation measures in some jurisdictions

**Laundromat:**
- No state-specific licensing
- Utility rates: favorable — Tennessee has some of the lowest commercial electric rates
  in the US (TVA service area)
- Low energy costs are a structural advantage for laundromat profitability

### Environmental Regulations
- TDEC is active but less aggressive than FL DEP
- Car wash wastewater: managed via NPDES permits; compliance costs are lower than FL
- **Risk level: LOW-MEDIUM** for car wash; LOW for janitorial and laundromat

### Employment Law
- **At-will employment:** YES — strong at-will state
- **Non-compete enforceability:** MODERATE — Tennessee enforces reasonable non-competes;
  recent legislative changes have clarified enforcement standards
- **Workers comp:** Required for 5+ employees. Moderate rates.
  - Janitorial (class 9014): 4-6% of payroll
  - Car wash: 2-3% of payroll
  - Laundromat: 1.5-2.5% of payroll (minimal labor intensity)
- **Contractor classification:** Moderate enforcement

### Metro Market Notes

**Nashville (Davidson, Williamson, Rutherford, Sumner, Wilson counties):**
- HIGHEST PRIORITY TN MARKET — one of the fastest-growing metros in the US (2020-2025)
- Commercial cleaning: strong demand from construction boom (post-construction cleaning),
  healthcare expansion, corporate relocations
- Car wash: significant express tunnel development 2020-2024; suburban corridors (Franklin,
  Brentwood, Murfreesboro, Mt. Juliet) have seen new express washes open; saturation
  becoming a concern in some submarkets
- Laundromat: demand driven by large renter population from population inflows; BUT
  gentrification risk is real in urban core neighborhoods (East Nashville, Germantown)
- Lease risk: Nashville commercial rents have risen 30-50% since 2020 in desirable areas

**Chattanooga:**
- Smaller market; lower competitive density; growing tech/remote-work base
- Car wash: moderate competition; less saturated than Nashville
- Good balance of growth with lower entry costs than Nashville

**Memphis:**
- Different economic character — more industrial, higher poverty rates, different demo profile
- Commercial cleaning demand from logistics/warehousing sector
- Laundromat demand is stable in working-class neighborhoods

**Knoxville:**
- University market; stable renter base
- Lower competitive intensity than Nashville

### Insurance Environment
- **Workers comp:** Moderate rates; lower than FL, comparable to GA
- **General liability:** Competitive market
- **Property:** Low hurricane/wind risk; tornado risk exists but manageable
- **Overall insurance cost pressure: LOW**

## NORTH CAROLINA (NC)

### Business-Friendliness: MEDIUM Risk (Moderate)

North Carolina has improved its business climate significantly (corporate tax cuts, pro-growth
legislature), but presents more regulatory complexity than AL or TN, particularly around
environmental compliance and employment law. Charlotte and Raleigh are major growth markets
with intense competition in service sectors. Minimum wage is trending toward increases.

### Minimum Wage Trajectory
- **Current rate:** $7.25/hr (state minimum equals federal; no state supplement as of 2026)
- **Trajectory:** WATCH — North Carolina legislature has shown some interest in gradual
  increases; political composition matters. No scheduled increase confirmed as of 2026,
  but given the state's political evolution, a $10-12 floor within 3-5 years is plausible.
- **Charlotte/Raleigh effective wages:** Market pressure pushes actual janitorial wages to
  $14-16/hr regardless of legal floor.
- **Risk:** Medium-term legislative risk if political composition of legislature shifts.

### Vertical-Specific Regulations

**Janitorial/Commercial Cleaning:**
- No state janitorial licensing requirement (beyond standard business registration)
- Charlotte and Raleigh have active commercial real estate markets with high cleanliness
  standards — clients often specify green cleaning or LEED compliance
- Workers comp: required for all employers with 3+ employees

**Car Wash:**
- NC DEQ (Department of Environmental Quality) is one of the more active in the region
- Stormwater regulations are meaningful; car wash operators must meet NC stormwater
  permitting requirements
- Water recycling systems are increasingly expected in new construction and renovation
  permits in metro areas
- Charlotte-area car wash operators have faced more rigorous DEQ oversight than typical
  in the Southeast

**Laundromat:**
- No state-specific licensing
- Duke Energy and Dominion Energy service territories; commercial electric rates are
  moderate (higher than TN/TVA, lower than FL)

### Environmental Regulations
- **NC DEQ is among the more active environmental regulators in the Southeast**
- Car wash wastewater: meaningful compliance costs; stormwater management requirements
  are more rigorous than AL, TN, and GA (though less stringent than FL)
- Hazardous materials: RCRA applies; NC has active state-level enforcement
- **Risk level: MEDIUM** for car wash; LOW-MEDIUM for janitorial; LOW for laundromat

### Employment Law
- **At-will employment:** YES
- **Non-compete enforceability:** MODERATE — North Carolina courts take a case-by-case
  approach; enforceability depends heavily on scope and reasonableness
- **Workers comp:** Required for 3+ employees. Rates are moderate.
  - Janitorial (class 9014): 5-7% of payroll — moderate; similar to GA
  - Car wash: 2-4% of payroll
- **Contractor classification:** NC has been active in enforcement; scrutinize businesses
  with high 1099 worker concentration
- **E-Verify:** North Carolina requires E-Verify for businesses with 25+ employees —
  relevant for larger janitorial operations

### Metro Market Notes

**Charlotte (Mecklenburg, Union, Cabarrus, York SC counties — the broader metro):**
- One of the fastest-growing metros in the Southeast; major financial/corporate sector
- Commercial cleaning: high demand; national chains (ABM Industries, Aramark) have large
  footprints; independent operators compete on price and service quality
- Car wash: Charlotte suburban corridors (Ballantyne, Matthews, Huntersville, Concord)
  have seen significant express tunnel development 2019-2024; saturation is a concern
  in specific high-traffic corridors
- Laundromat: strong demand in working-class and immigrant communities in east and west
  Charlotte; gentrification risk in some inner-ring neighborhoods

**Raleigh/Durham/Research Triangle:**
- Tech/biotech/university economy; high household income
- Commercial cleaning demand from biotech and pharma facilities (higher-spec cleaning)
- Car wash: growing market; saturation not yet at Charlotte levels
- Laundromat: strong demand from large student/renter population (NCCU, Duke, NC State, UNC)

**Asheville:**
- Tourist economy; hospitality commercial cleaning demand
- Much smaller market; lower competitive intensity

### Insurance Environment
- **Workers comp:** Moderate-to-elevated rates (workers comp fund is active; rates vary
  by class but generally in middle of Southeast range)
- **Hurricane/wind:** Less hurricane risk than FL/SC coast, but significant wind and storm
  exposure in coastal NC areas. Western NC does not face hurricane risk.
- **Property:** Moderate; tornado and severe storm risk in Piedmont areas
- **Overall insurance cost pressure: MEDIUM**

## SOUTH CAROLINA (SC)

### Business-Friendliness: LOW Risk (Favorable)

South Carolina is a pro-business state with low regulatory burden and favorable workers
comp rates. Charleston and the Greenville-Spartanburg corridor are growth markets. The
state has less population density than GA, NC, or FL, which means lower competitive
intensity in most service verticals — but also smaller total addressable markets.

### Minimum Wage Trajectory
- **Current rate:** $7.25/hr (federal minimum; South Carolina has no state minimum wage)
- **Trajectory:** VERY STABLE — no active legislation; legislature is strongly opposed
  to minimum wage mandates
- **Impact on janitorial:** Favorable. Charleston effective janitorial wages: $12-14/hr.
  Upstate SC (Greenville, Spartanburg): $11-13/hr.

### Vertical-Specific Regulations

**Janitorial/Commercial Cleaning:**
- No state licensing requirement
- Standard business registration
- Workers comp: required for all employers with 4+ employees

**Car Wash:**
- SC DHEC (Department of Health and Environmental Control) regulates water discharge
- Stormwater permit requirements exist but are generally less burdensome than NC/FL
- Some coastal municipalities (Charleston, Hilton Head) have more stringent water quality
  requirements

**Laundromat:**
- No state-specific licensing
- Electric rates: moderate (Duke Energy and Santee Cooper service territories)

### Environmental Regulations
- SC DHEC is less aggressive than FL DEP and NC DEQ
- Car wash: manageable compliance requirements
- Coastal development regulations (OCRM — Office of Coastal Resource Management) affect
  properties in beachfront/coastal zones — less relevant for janitorial/laundromat but
  relevant for car wash site selection
- **Risk level: LOW** for all three verticals in inland areas; MEDIUM for coastal

### Employment Law
- **At-will employment:** YES — strong at-will state
- **Non-compete enforceability:** HIGH — South Carolina courts enforce reasonable
  non-competes; one of the more employer-friendly states in the region
- **Workers comp:** Required for 4+ employees. Rates are favorable.
  - Janitorial (class 9014): 4-6% of payroll — among the lowest in the Southeast
  - Car wash: 2-3% of payroll
- **Contractor classification:** Standard federal rules; moderate state enforcement

### Metro Market Notes

**Charleston (Charleston, Berkeley, Dorchester counties):**
- Fast-growing; tourism + logistics economy
- Commercial cleaning demand from hospitality and port-related commercial real estate
- Car wash: growing market; lower saturation than Charlotte/Atlanta suburban corridors
- Laundromat: moderate demand; some gentrification risk in downtown/peninsula areas
- Coastal hurricane exposure is real — insurance costs are elevated vs. inland SC

**Greenville-Spartanburg (Upstate SC):**
- Manufacturing and BMW/Michelin corporate presence creates commercial cleaning demand
- Car wash: moderate competition; steady suburban growth
- Laundromat: working-class neighborhoods in both cities; stable renter base
- Inland location: lower hurricane risk; favorable insurance costs

**Columbia:**
- State capital/university market (USC)
- Moderate competitive density
- Stable economy; good laundromat demand from student/renter population

### Insurance Environment
- **Workers comp:** Favorable rates — among the lowest in the Southeast
- **General liability:** Competitive market; standard rates
- **Property / Hurricane:** Coastal SC (Charleston) has meaningful wind/hurricane risk.
  Inland SC: significantly lower risk.
- **Overall insurance cost pressure: LOW (inland) to MEDIUM (coastal)**

|-------|-----------------|---------------|--------------|-----------------|---------|
| GA    | LOW             | LOW           | MEDIUM       | LOW-MEDIUM      | LOW     |
| FL    | MEDIUM          | HIGH (rising) | HIGH         | MEDIUM-HIGH     | MEDIUM-HIGH |

| TN    | LOW             | LOW           | LOW-MEDIUM   | LOW-MEDIUM      | LOW     |

regulatory risk for service business acquisitions. Florida presents the highest risk,
primarily driven by rising minimum wage, high workers comp costs, hurricane insurance
exposure, and stricter environmental compliance for car wash operators.
