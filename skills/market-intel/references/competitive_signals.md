# Competitive Signals — Market Intel Reference

Framework for assessing competitive intensity for each vertical in the target geography
(GA, FL, AL, TN, NC, SC). Use this reference to determine whether a deal's local market
is THIN, MODERATE, or SATURATED — and to identify whether the business has a durable moat.

---

## How to Use This Reference

1. Identify the deal's vertical and metro area.
2. Apply the vertical-specific framework below to assess competitive density.
3. Rate the competitive density: THIN / MODERATE / SATURATED.
4. Assess whether the business has any moat signals.
5. Report findings in the Market Intel Report under "Competitive Density."

**Tools for competitive research:**
- Google Maps — search "[vertical] near [city, state]"; count competitors within radius
- Yelp — search by category in the zip code area
- BizBuySell — check for active listings in the same vertical/geography (active listings
  signal both competitive density AND market saturation)
- LinkedIn — check for PE-backed operators with offices in the metro
- IFA (International Franchise Association) directory — for franchise operator density
- Express Wash Concepts / ZIPS / Mister Car Wash store locator — for car wash saturation

## VERTICAL 1: JANITORIAL / COMMERCIAL CLEANING

### How to Assess Competitive Density

**Primary metric:** Number of commercial cleaning companies actively operating in the
metro area (not necessarily in the same sub-market as the acquisition target).

**Search approach:**
- Google Maps: search "commercial cleaning" or "janitorial service" near [city] → count
  unique results within a 15-mile radius of the target business
- Yelp: same search, count unique companies
- Remove duplicates (same company appearing under multiple names)
- Note national/franchise brands separately from independent operators

**Density Scale:**

| Count in Metro (15-mile radius) | Classification |
|-------------------------------|----------------|
| <15 independent operators     | THIN |
| 15-50 independent operators   | MODERATE |
| >50 independent operators     | SATURATED |

**Adjustment factors:**
- If 3+ national franchise brands (Jan-Pro, Coverall, ServiceMaster Clean, ABM) are
  actively operating in the metro → push classification one level higher (MODERATE → SATURATED)
- If the target business serves a specialty niche (medical, post-construction, industrial,
  cleanroom) → push classification one level lower (SATURATED → MODERATE)
- If the metro is large (Atlanta, Charlotte, Nashville) → use 20-mile radius and double
  the count thresholds

**Signals of saturation:**
- Google Maps shows 50+ results for "commercial cleaning" in the metro
- Multiple active BizBuySell listings for janitorial businesses in the same city
  (sellers list when the market is soft; multiple concurrent listings signal a difficult
  competitive or operational environment)
- National franchise operators (Jan-Pro, Coverall) are actively advertising in the metro
- Price-per-square-foot for commercial cleaning is below $0.06/sq ft (indicates heavy
  price competition)

**Thin market signals:**
- Fewer than 10 independent operators identifiable in the metro
- No major franchise operators present
- Target business serves a niche (medical, industrial, post-construction) with few
  specialized competitors
- Geographic isolation (rural, small city) that national operators have not prioritized

### Janitorial — Specific Risk: Customer Concentration Amplifier

Customer concentration and competitive density interact:
- A janitorial business with 1 anchor client representing 40% of revenue in a SATURATED
  market has **EXTREME exit risk**: if that client leaves, it will be replaced by a
  national brand competitor, not a loyal independent substitute
- A janitorial business with 1 anchor client representing 40% of revenue in a THIN market
  has **MODERATE exit risk**: client has fewer alternatives; switching costs are higher
- **Scoring rule:** Multiply customer concentration risk by competitive density risk when
  assessing total competitive exposure

### Janitorial — Metro-Specific Context

| Metro | Competitive Density | Notes |
|-------|--------------------|----- |
| Atlanta, GA | SATURATED | 200+ operators; national brands dominant; most competitive market in region |
| Charlotte, NC | SATURATED | Large market; ABM/Aramark present; heavy competition |
| Nashville, TN | MODERATE-SATURATED | Growing fast; reaching saturation; specialty niches less crowded |
| Jacksonville, FL | MODERATE | Large enough to support many operators; less franchise penetration than Atlanta |
| Birmingham, AL | MODERATE | Mid-size market; fewer national brands; better for independents |
| Raleigh/Durham, NC | MODERATE | University/tech market; growing demand; specialty niches available |
| Chattanooga, TN | THIN-MODERATE | Smaller market; lower national brand presence |
| Greenville, SC | THIN-MODERATE | Manufacturing corridor; cleaning demand real but fewer operators |
| Savannah, GA | THIN | Growing market; underserved relative to population growth |
| Secondary AL/TN/SC | THIN | Small cities; independent operators have strong local position |

## VERTICAL 2: CAR WASH

### How to Assess Competitive Density

**Primary metric for express tunnel:** Number of express tunnel car washes within a
5-mile radius of the target location.

This is the single most important data point for a car wash acquisition. Get it right.

**Search approach:**
- Google Maps: search "car wash" near the target address; filter by "car wash" category;
  identify and count express tunnel operations specifically within 5 miles
- Mister Car Wash store locator: mistercarwash.com/locations
- ZIPS Car Wash store locator: zipcarwash.com/locations
- Search "[city] express car wash" to find regionals that may not appear in Google Maps top results

**Density Scale — Express Tunnel:**

| Express Tunnels Within 5 Miles | Classification |
|-------------------------------|----------------|
| 0-1 express tunnels           | GREEN (THIN) |
| 2-3 express tunnels           | YELLOW (MODERATE) |
| 4+ express tunnels            | RED (SATURATED) |

**Adjustment factors:**
- In dense urban/suburban areas with high traffic counts (>30,000 VPD on adjacent road),
  the market can support more competition — adjust thresholds slightly upward
- In lower-density suburban or rural areas, even 2 express tunnels within 5 miles may
  represent saturation given the smaller addressable market

**For self-service and in-bay car washes:**
- Self-service competes primarily with self-service (not express tunnels — they serve
  different use cases)
- Count self-service bays within 3 miles for self-service density
- In-bay automatic: count in-bay automatic and express tunnels within 3 miles

**Membership saturation:**
- If 3+ operators within the same zip code offer unlimited monthly membership programs,
  membership margin compression is likely regardless of tunnel count
- Check: does the target's market have membership-based pricing from multiple competitors?
  If yes, the buyer should model expected pricing pressure on membership rates

### Car Wash — Signals of Saturation
- 4+ express tunnels within 5 miles (immediate red flag)
- Multiple express tunnels opened 2020-2024 in the same corridor (boom-era overbuilding)
- Google Maps shows strong clustering of car washes in the same strip-mall corridors
- Multiple active BizBuySell listings for car washes in the same metro (sellers trying to exit)
- PE-backed brands (Mister Car Wash, ZIPS, Magnolia, Tommy's, EG Group) have locations
  within 3 miles
- Target business has declining membership count or increasing membership churn rate

### Car Wash — Thin Market Signals (Green Flags)
- 0-1 express tunnels within 5 miles
- No PE-backed operators within the immediate competitive radius
- High traffic count at the site (>35,000 VPD) with limited competition
- Location in a growing suburban corridor where population is increasing faster than
  car wash supply (new subdivisions, new commercial development)
- Closest competing express tunnel is blocked by a meaningful geographic barrier
  (river, highway interchange, commercial dead zone)

### Car Wash — Metro-Specific Context

| Metro | Express Tunnel Density | Status |
|-------|----------------------|--------|
| Atlanta suburbs (Kennesaw, Marietta, Roswell, Duluth) | 3-6 per 5-mile radius | SATURATED |
| Nashville suburbs (Franklin, Brentwood, Murfreesboro) | 3-5 per 5-mile radius | SATURATED in core suburbs |
| Charlotte suburbs (Ballantyne, Matthews, Huntersville) | 3-5 per 5-mile radius | SATURATED in core suburbs |
| Jacksonville, FL (suburban corridors) | 2-3 per 5-mile radius | MODERATE-SATURATED |
| Tampa/Orlando suburbs | 4-7 per 5-mile radius | VERY SATURATED |
| Birmingham, AL | 1-3 per 5-mile radius | MODERATE |
| Chattanooga, TN | 1-2 per 5-mile radius | THIN-MODERATE |
| Secondary AL, SC, TN cities | 0-1 per 5-mile radius | THIN-GREEN |
| Rural Southeast | 0-1 per 10+ mile radius | THIN-GREEN |

## VERTICAL 3: LAUNDROMAT

### How to Assess Competitive Density

**Primary metric:** Ratio of laundromats to renter households in the immediate zip code.

**Why renters?** Laundromats primarily serve households without in-unit washer/dryer access.
In-unit W/D penetration is strongly correlated with homeownership and higher-income rental
housing. The relevant customer base is renters (and specifically cost-conscious renters)
— not the total population.

**Search approach:**
- Google Maps: search "laundromat" near the target zip code; count unique results
- Yelp: "Laundromats" category in the zip code
- USPS / census data: total renter-occupied housing units in the zip code
  (use census.gov ACS data or a free proxy like unitedstateszipcodes.org)

**Density Scale:**

| Laundromats per Renter Households | Classification |
|----------------------------------|----------------|
| 1 laundromat per 4,000-6,000 renter HH | GREEN (THIN) |
| 1 laundromat per 2,000-4,000 renter HH | YELLOW (MODERATE) |
| 1 laundromat per <2,000 renter HH | RED (SATURATED) |

**Additional density factors:**
- **In-unit W/D penetration:** If the neighborhood has high in-unit W/D penetration
  (typical of newer apartment buildings or gentrifying areas), the effective addressable
  market is smaller than total renter count suggests. Signs: new apartment buildings
  advertising "washer/dryer included," high median rents in the area.
- **WDF (Wash-Dry-Fold) service competitors:** Count WDF-only drop-off businesses
  separately from coin/card self-service laundromats — they serve overlapping customer
  needs; density of WDF services compresses the WDF revenue opportunity for the target

### Laundromat — Declining Renter Population (Gentrification Detector)

The renter population declining or converting to homeowners is a different risk from
competitive density — it's demand erosion, not competition.

**Signals of declining renter population:**
- Median home value increase >30% in 5 years (check Zillow/Redfin neighborhood data)
- New condo development announcements in the immediate area
- A major anchor tenant (Whole Foods, Target) opening within 0.5 miles
- City investment in streetscape / public transit in the neighborhood
- Demographic shift data from census (increasing median income, declining poverty rate)

**Combine this with lease assessment:**
- Declining renter population + short lease (< 5 years remaining) = HIGH RISK
- Declining renter population + long lease (10+ years including renewals) = MODERATE RISK
  (lease provides runway but demand may erode before lease expires)

### Laundromat — Signals of Saturation
- 1 laundromat per <2,000 renter households in the zip code
- Multiple laundromats visible within 0.25 miles of the target
- Competing laundromat has recently upgraded to card/app payment systems (competitive
  response to market pressure)
- Target business revenue is flat or declining over 3+ years (demand saturation signal)

### Laundromat — Thin Market Signals (Green Flags)
- 1 laundromat per 4,000+ renter households in the zip code
- Stable or growing renter population in the area
- No WDF competitors in the zip code
- Near a large apartment complex without in-unit W/D connections
- Near a university or college student population
- Working-class neighborhood with high household density (row houses, small apartments)
- Coin-only laundromat → immediate conversion to card system = 20-30% revenue uplift
  opportunity (competitive advantage over coin-only neighbors)

### Laundromat — Metro-Specific Context

| Area | Laundromat Environment | Notes |
|------|----------------------|-------|
| Atlanta intown (Decatur, East Atlanta, College Park) | MODERATE | Renter density is high; gentrification variable by specific neighborhood |
| Nashville urban core (East Nashville, Germantown) | MODERATE + HIGH gentrification risk | Population growth is fast; gentrification is accelerating |
| Birmingham working-class neighborhoods | THIN-MODERATE | Stable renter base; limited new competition |
| Charlotte working-class east/west corridors | MODERATE | Diverse renter base; some gentrification pressure in select areas |
| Jacksonville Northside/Westside | THIN | Underserved; working-class renters; less development pressure |
| Greenville SC working-class | THIN-MODERATE | Smaller market; stable renter base |
| Secondary AL/TN/SC cities | THIN | Best opportunities; limited competition |

## Competitive Moat Signals — All Verticals

A competitive moat is a durable advantage that protects the business from new entrants
or existing competitors. Strong moats justify premium valuations; absent moats require
discounted valuations (and extra caution on competitive density).

### Moat Type 1: Long-Term Anchor Contracts (Janitorial)
- **Definition:** Multi-year (3-5 year) cleaning contracts with anchor clients that
  represent 20-40% of revenue
- **Strength:** HIGH — contracts create revenue certainty, switching costs for clients,
  and revenue predictability that supports absentee ownership
- **Evidence required:** Actual contract review; not broker verbal claims
- **Qualification:** Only counts if contracts have (a) specific duration remaining,
  (b) renewal clauses, and (c) have survived at least one prior renewal
- **Caution:** Customer concentration risk coexists with anchor contract moat; single
  anchor over 40% of revenue is still dangerous even with a contract

### Moat Type 2: Premium Location (Car Wash / Laundromat)
- **Definition:** Location with high traffic count, limited competitors in proximity,
  and barriers to new competitive entry nearby
- **Strength:** HIGH for car wash; MEDIUM-HIGH for laundromat
- **Evidence required:**
  - Car wash: traffic count >40,000 VPD AND nearest competitor >3 miles
  - Laundromat: only laundromat in the zip code AND zip code has 2,000+ renter households
- **Qualification:** The moat is geographic — it exists as long as no new competitor
  enters within the competitive radius. The buyer should assess whether a competitor
  could realistically enter (available real estate, zoning, cost of entry).

### Moat Type 3: Established Local Reputation / Relationships (Janitorial)
- **Definition:** Business has served the same clients for 5+ years; clients have personal
  relationships with key employees or the owner; referral network is active
- **Strength:** MEDIUM — moderately sticky but transferable if the owner-relationship is
  the sole basis of the moat (risk: owner-dependent)
- **Evidence required:**
  - Average client tenure of 3+ years
  - Top clients have renewed contracts multiple times
  - Referral-sourced new clients (not just broker-generated)
- **Qualification:** If the moat is owner-dependency (clients stay because of the owner's
  personal relationship), the moat disappears at acquisition. This is actually a RED FLAG,
  not a moat, for an absentee buyer.

### Moat Type 4: Proprietary Route or Service Territory (Laundromat / Janitorial)
- **Definition:** The business owns or controls access to a specific geographic service
  territory, either through exclusive contracts or proprietary customer relationships
  that are formally assigned and transferable
- **Strength:** MEDIUM — depends on enforceability and whether competitors can enter the
  same territory
- **Evidence required:** Written exclusivity agreements, route documentation, or
  established customer base in a defined territory with verified exclusivity provisions
- **Qualification:** Verbal exclusivity claims from brokers do not constitute a moat.
  Verify with actual documentation.

### Moat Type 5: Brand and Local Identity (Laundromat / Car Wash)
- **Definition:** Long-established (10+ year) local business with strong Google reviews
  (4.0+ stars, 50+ reviews), repeat customer base, and recognizable local brand
- **Strength:** LOW-MEDIUM — meaningful in small markets; less impactful in large
  competitive metros where alternatives are readily available
- **Evidence required:** Google Maps reviews count and quality; years in operation; Yelp
  presence; local community recognition

## Competitive Density Summary Matrix

| Vertical | Key Metric | THIN | MODERATE | SATURATED |
|----------|-----------|------|----------|-----------|
| Janitorial | Operators in metro (15-mi) | <15 | 15-50 | >50 |
| Car Wash | Express tunnels within 5 mi | 0-1 | 2-3 | 4+ |
| Laundromat | Laundromats per renter HH | 1:4,000+ | 1:2,000-4,000 | 1:<2,000 |

**Verdict mapping:**
- THIN → supports CAUTION or GO verdict (competitive environment is favorable)
- MODERATE → neutral; other factors determine verdict
- SATURATED → supports CAUTION or AVOID verdict (competitive environment is unfavorable)
  unless the specific business has a demonstrable, durable moat
