# Industry Trends — Market Intel Reference

Covers macro-level trend analysis for the three target verticals:
janitorial/commercial cleaning, car wash, and laundromat.

Use the relevant vertical section when running market intel for a deal.
Data reflects 2025-2026 conditions. Industry dynamics change — ground-truth with current
sources where possible, particularly for consolidation activity.

---

## VERTICAL 1: JANITORIAL / COMMERCIAL CLEANING

### Industry Overview
- **Market size:** ~$60-65 billion US addressable market (commercial + industrial cleaning)
- **Growth rate:** ~4-5% CAGR (2023-2028 projections)
- **Trend: GROWING** — structural demand tailwinds are real and durable
- **Fragmentation:** Extremely fragmented. Top 10 players hold less than 20% of market.
  The vast majority are independent operators with under $5M in revenue.

### Why It's Growing

**Post-COVID hygiene baseline shift:** The COVID-19 pandemic permanently raised the
minimum cleaning standards expected by commercial tenants, property managers, and building
owners. Quarterly deep cleans have become baseline in many office contracts. Healthcare
adjacency (medical offices, urgent care, dental) is growing fast.

**Commercial real estate recovery (selective):** While traditional office occupancy has
not fully recovered post-COVID, industrial, logistics, healthcare, and government facility
cleaning demand has grown significantly to compensate. Cleaning companies that diversified
away from pure-office contracts are growing faster.

**Outsourcing trend:** Companies continue to outsource non-core functions. In-house
cleaning staff are being replaced by contract cleaning services across all facility types.
This expands the addressable market for independent operators.

**Green cleaning premium:** EPA Safer Choice certified, LEED-compatible, and "green"
cleaning services command a 10-20% price premium in commercial markets, particularly in
tech, healthcare, and corporate office segments. Operators with green certification can
defend margins better than those competing on price alone.

### Consolidation / Roll-Up Risk: HIGH

This is the single most important macro risk for independent janitorial operators.

**PE-backed platforms operating in the Southeast:**
- Coverall: franchise-based; heavy Southeast presence
- Jan-Pro: franchise-based; aggressive market expansion
- Bonus Building Care: regional
- ABM Industries (public): commercial cleaning, facilities services; $3B+ revenue; active
  in all Southeast metros
- Aramark / Sodexo / ISS: primarily large commercial/institutional accounts

**What PE roll-up means for independents:**
- National operators compete on scale, tech platforms, and price — independents must
  compete on service quality and relationships
- Small independents ($250K-$1M revenue) are in the "too small to acquire" zone for PE
  but in the direct competitive path of franchise operators
- Clients are increasingly asking for "enterprise" providers; multi-location accounts may
  prefer national operators for uniformity of service
- **Risk to buyer:** Selling price premiums for large cleaning businesses (>$2M revenue)
  are compressing as PE consolidation progresses; smaller operators face price pressure
  from franchisees who have lower cost structures through economies of scale

**Consolidation verdict:** YES — this vertical IS being rolled up at the upper end.
Independent operators in the $250K-$2M range are not direct acquisition targets yet,
but they compete against franchise operators who benefit from PE capital. This is a
WATCH-level risk, not immediate, but it will intensify over 5-10 years.

### Labor Market Pressure: HIGH

- **Annual turnover rate: 100-200%** in commercial cleaning — among the highest of any
  industry. A typical janitorial business will replace its entire workforce 1-2 times
  per year.
- **Root causes:** Low wages, physical demands, irregular hours, part-time/split-shift
  structures, immigration enforcement sensitivity
- **Impact on operations:** High training cost, inconsistent service quality, management
  burden, client complaints
- **Absentee viability challenge:** High turnover is the primary reason commercial
  cleaning scores lower on absentee viability than car wash or laundromat. Without
  a strong on-site manager who can handle constant hiring and training, operations
  degrade rapidly.
- **Mitigation:** Higher-than-market wages (competitive pay) + strong crew leaders +
  automated scheduling software (Method, Swept, ZenMaid) can reduce turnover to 60-80%
  annually in well-run shops.

### Automation / Technology Risk: LOW-MEDIUM (near-term), MEDIUM-HIGH (5-10 year)

**Current automation reality:** Robotic floor scrubbers (Brain Corp, Tennant) are
deployed in large commercial spaces (airports, large retail, warehouses). For the
$250K-$2M independent janitorial operator, these are not an immediate competitive threat.

**Near-term tech impact:**
- Scheduling/routing software (Swept, Method, Jobber) reduces management overhead
  by 20-30% — operators using tech have an advantage
- GPS tracking and real-time reporting apps are increasingly expected by commercial clients
- Employee app-based training and time tracking reduce HR administrative burden

**Longer-term (5-10 year) risk:** As robotic cleaning technology scales and costs drop,
larger commercial accounts may increasingly use automation for routine floor/surface work.
This will commoditize the lower end of commercial cleaning. Operators in specialty niches
(medical, post-construction, hazmat) have longer runways.

**Assessment for buyer:** Not an immediate threat for the $250K-$1.5M acquisition range,
but the buyer should understand the trajectory. Businesses in specialty verticals
(medical, post-construction, industrial) have better long-term moats than generic office
cleaning businesses.

### Key Risks
1. **Labor turnover — #1 operational risk** for absentee owners
2. **Customer concentration** — single large account >30% of revenue is existential risk
3. **Owner-dependency** — seller may be the key relationship holder for top accounts
4. **Price-sensitive clients** — commercial cleaning is commoditized; price pressure is constant
5. **National competitor entry** — franchise operators can undercut pricing in any market
6. **Wage inflation** — even without legislative mandates, market wages are rising

### Southeast-Specific Context
- Atlanta and Charlotte are the most competitive markets; independent operators face the
  most pressure from national brands
- Birmingham, Nashville secondary, Chattanooga, and SC secondary markets have lower
  national chain penetration — more favorable for independents
- Healthcare cleaning demand is growing in all Southeast metros due to hospital expansion
- Government/institutional cleaning (schools, courts, government facilities) is a
  relatively stable niche with long contract terms — valuable if the target business has it

## VERTICAL 2: CAR WASH

### Industry Overview
- **Market size:** ~$15-17 billion US addressable market
- **Growth rate:** ~5-7% CAGR pre-2022 (slowing as saturation increases); ~3-4% CAGR
  forward projections (2024-2028)
- **Trend: STABLE with pockets of DECLINING** — heavily dependent on local market conditions
- **Fragmentation:** Moderately fragmented; but PE consolidation is more advanced than
  janitorial. Top operators include ZIPS Car Wash, Mister Car Wash (public company),
  Magnolia Car Wash, and many regional chains.

### The Express Tunnel Boom and Bust Cycle (2019-2024)

**The boom:** Between 2019 and 2023, the express tunnel car wash model attracted massive
PE investment. The thesis: high-volume, subscription-based car washing with low labor
intensity generates strong, predictable cash flows. Mister Car Wash went public in 2021.
PE-backed operators (ZIPS, Magnolia, Hoffman's, Rainstorm, Xponential) aggressively
expanded, often opening in the same submarkets simultaneously.

**The reality:** Significant overbuilding occurred in suburban markets in GA, FL, TN, and
NC. In some suburban corridors (Kennesaw/Marietta GA, Brentwood/Franklin TN, Ballantyne
NC, suburban Jacksonville FL), 3-6 express tunnels now compete within a 3-5 mile radius.

**The hangover (2023-2025):** Membership churn has increased as consumers have more
options. Revenue per member has declined as operators discount to retain members.
Several PE-backed operators have experienced financial stress; some have closed
underperforming locations or sold. ZIPS Car Wash filed for bankruptcy in early 2024.

**Impact on acquisition targets:** Express tunnel businesses acquired at 3.0x-4.5x SDE
in 2021-2022 are worth less today in saturated markets. Verify current membership count,
churn rate, and revenue trends (not just trailing 12 months — look at the trend over
24-36 months).

### Car Wash Model Types and Their Trend Outlook

**Express Tunnel (automated, subscription-focused):**
- Peak overbuilding risk; saturation is the dominant story in major metros
- Membership model is under pressure as market fragments
- Still the best absentee model if the specific location has strong competitive position
- Trend in saturated markets: DECLINING; in undersaturated locations: STABLE

**Full-Service / Flex Serve:**
- Labor-intensive; difficult absentee model
- Trend: DECLINING as express tunnels take market share

**Self-Service (coin/credit card activated):**
- Slow obsolescence as consumers shift to express tunnel model
- Still relevant in rural and secondary markets; provides "clean my truck/dog" use cases
  that express tunnels don't serve
- Trend: DECLINING (long, slow decline — 10-15 year runway in secondary markets)

**In-Bay Automatic (touchless/rollover):**
- Lower capex than express tunnel; smaller footprint
- Serves rural/secondary markets; less competition from PE-backed express operators
- Trend: STABLE in secondary markets

### Membership Model Saturation

The membership subscription model (unlimited monthly wash for ~$19-35/month) is now
present in virtually every major metro. In some markets, every express tunnel within a
5-mile radius has an unlimited membership offer. This creates:

- **Race to the bottom on membership pricing** — operators discount to attract/retain
  members, compressing margins
- **High churn** — members cancel when they find a cheaper option; typical monthly churn
  is 4-8% in competitive markets (vs. 2-3% in underserved markets)
- **Revenue per car declining** — even as visit volume holds, effective revenue per car
  is under pressure from deep discount membership plans

**Implication for buyer:** Membership count at acquisition is less meaningful than
membership retention rate, average revenue per member, and trend over time. A business
with 1,000 members but 8% monthly churn is structurally weaker than one with 700 members
and 2% monthly churn.

### Water Recycling Mandates

**Trend:** Increasing, especially in water-stressed regions and progressive metro areas.
Florida, parts of NC, and some CA municipalities have moved toward requiring water
recycling systems for car wash operations.

**Current Southeast status (2025-2026):**
- No statewide mandates in GA, AL, TN, SC — but some metro-level requirements may exist
- Florida (particularly South FL and Tampa Bay corridor): increasing pressure; some
  municipalities require recycling for new construction
- NC: growing regulatory pressure, particularly in Charlotte/Raleigh metro areas

**Cost of retrofitting water recycling:** $50,000-$150,000 depending on system scale.
This is a material capex item. If a target does not have water recycling and the local
regulatory environment is trending toward mandates, build this into the deal model.

### Equipment Capex Risk

Express tunnel equipment has a useful life of approximately 10-15 years with proper
maintenance. Equipment installed in the 2018-2022 boom period will require partial
replacement or overhaul by the late 2020s to early 2030s.

**Key equipment categories:**
- Conveyor system: $200,000-$500,000 to replace
- Chemical delivery systems: $30,000-$100,000
- Blowers / dryer systems: $50,000-$150,000
- POS / RFID membership systems: $20,000-$50,000

**Buyer implication:** Always assess equipment age and maintenance records. A tunnel
installed in 2019 has 5-8 years of useful life remaining assuming proper maintenance —
but deferred maintenance accelerates replacement timelines. Reserve adequate capex
in the acquisition model.

### Key Risks
1. **Market saturation** — #1 risk for most metro express tunnel locations
2. **Membership churn** — eroding revenue predictability
3. **Equipment capex** — substantial replacement costs loom for 2019-2023 vintage equipment
4. **PE competitor pricing power** — national chains can cross-subsidize markets
5. **Water recycling mandates** — increasing regulatory cost in some markets
6. **Chemical cost inflation** — detergents, waxes, and treatment chemicals have experienced
   15-25% cost increases since 2021

### Southeast-Specific Context
- Atlanta suburbs, Nashville suburbs, and Charlotte suburbs are among the most heavily
  saturated express tunnel markets in the Southeast
- Jacksonville FL, Birmingham AL, and secondary TN markets (Chattanooga, Knoxville) are
  less saturated and represent better opportunities
- Rural and small-city markets in AL, SC, and eastern TN have almost no express tunnel
  competition — highest-opportunity geography for car wash acquisitions
- Self-service car washes in rural Southeast markets still generate reliable cash flow
  with minimal management overhead

## VERTICAL 3: LAUNDROMAT

### Industry Overview
- **Market size:** ~$5 billion US (self-service laundromat, excluding commercial laundry)
- **Growth rate:** ~2-4% CAGR (2023-2028 projections) — modest but stable
- **Trend: STABLE** — structural demand is persistent; limited new supply
- **Fragmentation:** EXTREMELY fragmented — the vast majority are single-location
  independent operators. PE consolidation is in early innings relative to car wash
  and janitorial.

### Why the Laundromat Vertical Is Attractive Right Now

**PE consolidation has not yet hit this space hard.** Unlike janitorial (where national
franchise chains are everywhere) or car wash (where PE-backed express tunnels are
overbuilt in many markets), laundromats remain primarily mom-and-pop businesses. This
means:
- Acquisitions are at fundamentals-based multiples, not PE-inflated ones
- Operators who professionalize (card systems, remote monitoring, WDF services) can
  outperform legacy competitors significantly
- The "rollup window" may be 5-10 years before institutional capital arrives at scale

**Persistent demand from renters:** Laundromats serve households without in-unit
washer/dryer access — primarily renters, often in working-class and immigrant communities.
In Southeast secondary and tertiary markets, this renter population is stable and growing.

**Technology upgrade opportunity:** Many existing laundromats still run on coin-only
systems. Converting to card/app payment systems (CryptoPay, Laundromat Partner, Speed
Queen Connect) drives 20-30% revenue increases in most operations by:
- Capturing customers who don't carry coins
- Enabling remote pricing and revenue monitoring
- Reducing coin collection overhead
- Reducing theft risk

**Commercial laundry adjacency:** Laundromats can add significant revenue by serving
commercial accounts (hotels, restaurants, gyms, spas, nursing homes) with wash-dry-fold
(WDF) or drop-off services. Commercial WDF accounts at $0.90-$1.50/lb represent 2-3x
the effective revenue rate of self-service washing. This is an underutilized growth lever
in most independent laundromats.

**Energy efficiency upgrade story:** Equipment installed before 2015 is typically 35-45%
less energy-efficient than current-generation Speed Queen, Maytag Commercial, or Electrolux
Professional washers and dryers. An operator who acquires an underinvested laundromat and
does a phased equipment upgrade can both improve the customer experience and reduce utility
costs as a percentage of revenue.

### Laundromat Business Models

**Unattended (coin/card, no staff):**
- Highest absentee viability; minimal labor overhead
- Revenue verification is the primary challenge (coin-only = difficult to audit without
  water/utility correlation)
- Card/app payment systems solve the revenue verification problem
- Typical SDE margins: 25-40% of revenue in well-run operations

**Attended (part-time attendant):**
- Adds labor cost; reduces some security risk; enables WDF service
- More manageable absentee model than janitorial or full-service car wash
- Revenue verification is somewhat easier (attendant can track transactions)

**Full-service WDF (wash-dry-fold):**
- Higher revenue potential; more complex operations
- Requires reliable staff for folding/sorting; not truly unattended
- SDE multiples are lower (2.0x-3.5x) because of labor dependency

### Gentrification Risk

**This is the #2 risk after lease risk for urban laundromats.**

In gentrifying neighborhoods, laundromats face two existential threats:
1. **Rent displacement:** Landlords raise rents dramatically or convert buildings as
   neighborhoods gentrify. Long-term leases with renewal options are the primary
   protection.
2. **Customer base erosion:** As income levels rise in a neighborhood, renters are
   replaced by homeowners or higher-income renters who have in-unit washer/dryer access.
   This directly reduces the laundromat's addressable customer base over time.

**Gentrification-exposed markets in the Southeast:**
- Nashville urban core (East Nashville, Germantown, The Gulch area)
- Atlanta intown neighborhoods (Old Fourth Ward, West End, East Point — accelerating)
- Charlotte inner-ring neighborhoods (NoDa, Plaza Midwood, Wesley Heights)
- Charleston peninsula (rapidly gentrifying — significant displacement already underway)
- Durham, NC (Central Park area, Old North Durham)

**Assessment framework:** If a laundromat is located in a neighborhood where:
- A new Whole Foods / Trader Joe's has opened within 0.5 miles
- The median home value has increased >30% in 5 years
- Newer apartment buildings are being constructed with in-unit W/D standard
... then gentrification risk is HIGH and must be reflected in the deal model.

**Protection:** Only gentrification-exposed laundromats with 10+ year remaining lease
(including renewal options) have adequate protection for an acquisition investment.

### Lease Risk

**Lease is the #1 risk for laundromats.** Unlike a janitorial business (which can
theoretically operate from a home office) or a car wash (where the facility is the
business and the real estate question is about ownership vs. lease), a laundromat IS
the facility. Moving a laundromat is essentially starting over.

**Minimum lease requirements for a laundromat acquisition:**
- At least 7-10 years remaining lease term (including exercisable renewal options)
- Renewal options should be at fixed or formulaic (CPI-adjusted) rates — avoid leases
  where renewal terms are "to be negotiated" (landlord has all leverage at renewal)
- Right of first refusal to purchase the building if possible
- Sublease/assignment provisions (can buyer sell the business and transfer the lease?)

**Red flags:**
- Less than 5 years remaining with no renewal option
- Landlord is an estate or institutional investor (more likely to redevelop or raise rents)
- Month-to-month lease (immediate risk)
- Lease renewal terms subject to negotiation (no fixed rate)

### Coin Collection and Revenue Verification

For coin-only laundromats, revenue is extremely difficult to independently verify:
- No POS system; no electronic records
- Revenue can be understated (seller collects more coin than reported) or overstated
  (seller inflates reported revenue to justify valuation)
- The primary verification method is utility bill correlation:
  - Water usage × cost per load = estimated load count
  - Electric usage × cost per load = estimated load count cross-check
  - Cross-check with reported revenue at market rate per load

**Card/app payment systems completely solve this problem** — every transaction is
electronically recorded. A laundromat with a modern payment system is dramatically
easier to underwrite than a coin-only laundromat.

### Key Risks
1. **Lease risk** — #1 risk; inadequate lease terms can make the business worthless
2. **Gentrification / customer base erosion** — demographic shift over lease term
3. **Revenue verification** (coin-only) — audit trail problem
4. **Equipment obsolescence** — aging machines in underinvested operations
5. **Utility cost increases** — energy and water rates are variable costs
6. **Neighborhood safety/crime** — vandalism and security incidents impact operations

### Southeast-Specific Context
- Birmingham, Nashville (secondary neighborhoods), Jacksonville, and secondary SC and GA
  markets have stable working-class renter populations — best geographic fit for laundromat
- Urban cores in Atlanta, Charlotte, and Nashville face the highest gentrification risk
- Coin laundry in rural AL, rural NC Piedmont, and rural SC is still primarily coin-only —
  conversion to card systems is a near-term value creation opportunity
- Commercial WDF adjacency is most viable in markets with hospitality/restaurant density
  (Charleston SC, Nashville, Jacksonville, Savannah GA)

## Summary — Vertical Trend Matrix

| Vertical | Macro Trend | Consolidation Risk | Labor Risk | Absentee Viability | Key Macro Risk |
|----------|-------------|-------------------|------------|-------------------|----------------|
| Janitorial | GROWING | HIGH (franchises) | HIGH | MEDIUM | Labor + consolidation |
| Car Wash | STABLE/DECLINING (market-specific) | HIGH (PE-backed) | LOW | HIGH | Overbuilding + capex |
| Laundromat | STABLE | LOW (window open) | LOW-MEDIUM | HIGH | Lease + gentrification |

**Bottom line:**
- **Janitorial** is the highest-growth vertical but carries the highest operational complexity
  for an absentee owner due to labor dynamics.
- **Car wash** has the best absentee profile but requires careful location analysis — market
  saturation is deal-specific and can make or break an acquisition.
- **Laundromat** is the most straightforward absentee model in fundamentals but lease risk and
  gentrification require careful underwriting. Technology upgrades represent real, near-term
  value creation opportunity.
