A 50% SDE margin is above the suspicious threshold of 50% noted in the behavioral reads reference, which states: "SDE margin > 50% (underreporting lease cost or not maintaining machines)." This warrants careful verification of reported expenses. Either the store has exceptionally low rent and utility costs, or expenses are being understated, or the SDE add-backs are aggressive.

**Revenue per square foot:** $210,000 / 2,800 sq ft = $75/sq ft. The benchmark for self-service only in a strong location is $20-$30/sq ft. Either the square footage is incorrect, the revenue is inflated, or this is a genuinely exceptional location (unlikely at this magnitude of deviation).

For washers, the average benchmark is $15-$25/day; for dryers, $8-$15/day. A blended $11/machine/day is on the lower end of reasonable, which actually introduces an inconsistency with the $75/sq ft revenue figure. This suggests the square footage may be well-utilized but the per-machine yields are modest -- plausible for a coin-only operation with moderate vend prices.

**Verdict: The asking price is RICH relative to what can be justified given the coin-only revenue verification risk. A fair price based on verified fundamentals would be $290,000-$320,000.**

## 4. SELLER-SIDE DECODING

### What They Are Emphasizing and Why

**"Stable neighborhood business with loyal customer base."** The word "stable" is used when they cannot say "growing." This signals flat or potentially declining revenue. "Loyal customer base" for a laundromat is a meaningless phrase -- laundromat customers are loyal to proximity and price, not to a brand or relationship. The broker is using emotional language to paper over the lack of growth story.

**"Perfect turnkey investment."** The seller playbook identifies "turnkey" as a term that "rarely means what buyers think." For a laundromat, "turnkey" should mean: card/app system with remote monitoring, formal maintenance contracts, security cameras, and no owner involvement needed. This store is coin-only with no remote monitoring capability. It is not turnkey for an absentee investor -- it is coin-collection-dependent, which requires regular physical presence.

**"The current owner checks in twice a week for about an hour each time."** This framing is designed to signal low owner involvement. But notice what it does not say: it does not mention what happens when a machine breaks down, who empties the coin boxes, who handles the change machine, who cleans the facility, or who monitors security. The part-time attendant at 20 hours/week likely handles cleaning and basic customer service, but machine maintenance and coin collection are probably the owner's "twice a week" visits. For an absentee buyer, these functions must be formalized and contracted.

### What They Are Likely Not Telling You

1. **Revenue is unverifiable.** Coin-only laundromats produce no transaction logs. The $210,000 revenue figure could be accurate, inflated, or deflated (for tax purposes, then re-inflated for sale). Without card/app transaction data, the only verification path is utility bill cross-referencing. The broker has not volunteered utility bills.

2. **Equipment condition details are vague.** "Most replaced within last 5 years" is deliberately imprecise. "Most" could mean 60% or 90%. "Replaced" could mean brand-new commercial machines or refurbished units. The 5-year claim may refer to some machines while others are 10-12 years old. Without serial numbers and maintenance logs, this claim is unverifiable.

3. **The lease situation.** Four years remaining with one 5-year renewal option is 9 years maximum. The broker is not emphasizing this because it is not a selling point. They mention it only because a buyer would ask. They are not mentioning rent escalation clauses, CAM charges, assignment provisions, or the landlord's long-term plans for the property.

4. **Why no card system after 17 years?** A store that has been operating since 2009 and still has no card or app payment system is either deliberately keeping things opaque (convenient for underreporting revenue to the IRS and overreporting to buyers) or the owner simply under-invested in technology. Either interpretation warrants investigation.

### Add-Back Red Flags

No SDE add-back schedule has been provided. The $105,000 SDE figure is presented without supporting detail. At a 50% SDE margin ($105,000 / $210,000), this number is at the upper boundary of plausibility for an unattended self-service store (benchmark: 30-45%). Either:

- The reported expenses are genuinely low (low rent, efficient equipment, minimal maintenance spend) -- possible but must be verified
- Legitimate expenses are being excluded from the P&L (deferred maintenance, unreported attendant costs, personal use of business funds reversed)
- The SDE add-backs are aggressive (e.g., adding back "non-recurring" equipment repairs that actually recur annually)

**You cannot trust this SDE until you have three years of tax returns and 24 months of utility bills in hand.**

### The "Retirement" Decode

The owner is 68 years old and states retirement as the reason for selling. This is one of the more credible retirement stories -- 68 is a natural retirement age, and 17 years of operating a laundromat is a long run. The willingness to carry 20% seller financing at 7% is consistent with a genuine retiree who believes the business will continue to perform (they are betting $77,000 of their own money on it).

However, even genuine retirees can be selling because they see problems ahead: a lease renewal negotiation they do not want to handle, equipment that is approaching end-of-life, or demographic changes in the neighborhood they have watched develop. The retirement may be real AND the timing may be strategic.

## 7. RED FLAGS (Ranked by Severity)

**[CRITICAL] Coin-only operation makes revenue unverifiable.**
This is the single most important risk factor. The $210,000 revenue and $105,000 SDE are based entirely on the seller's representation. Coin-operated machines produce no transaction logs. The seller could claim any number, and there is no machine-generated data to check. The only independent verification method is cross-referencing utility bills (water, gas, electric) against implied machine usage. If the seller cannot or will not produce 24 months of utility bills, this deal should be abandoned. Even if utility bills are provided, the verification is directional, not precise. Budget $25,000-$40,000 for a card/app system retrofit immediately post-close to eliminate this blindspot going forward.

**[CRITICAL] SDE margin of 50% exceeds the plausible range of 30-45%.**
The benchmark SDE margin for unattended self-service laundromats is 30-45% of revenue. At 50%, either the operating costs are genuinely below average (possible if rent is very low), or expenses are being understated, or SDE add-backs are aggressive. This requires line-by-line examination of the P&L. If expenses are being deferred (particularly equipment maintenance), the true SDE is lower and the true multiple is higher.

**[CRITICAL] Revenue per square foot of $75/sq ft far exceeds benchmark of $20-$30/sq ft.**
At $210,000 revenue in 2,800 sq ft, the revenue per square foot is $75. Even the premium full-service benchmark tops out at $55/sq ft. This magnitude of deviation is either an error in the reported square footage, an error in the reported revenue, or a genuinely exceptional (and suspicious) performance level. Demand independent verification of both the square footage (via lease document) and the revenue (via utility cross-check).

**[MODERATE] "Most replaced within last 5 years" is vague language requiring verification.**
"Most" is not "all." This language is designed to create the impression of newer equipment while leaving room for older machines in the mix. For 28 washers and 24 dryers, every machine needs to be documented: brand, model, serial number, installation date, and last service date. Speed Queen and Dexter encode the manufacture date in the serial number. If 30-40% of machines turn out to be 10+ years old, the equipment value is materially lower than implied, and a partial retool ($50,000-$150,000) may be needed within 3-5 years.

**[MODERATE] Lease has only 4 years remaining plus one 5-year renewal option (9 years maximum).**
The laundromat industry guide states the minimum acceptable lease term is 7 years, with 10+ years being ideal. At 4 years remaining on the base term, the buyer is already below the minimum before any equipment investment is made. The single 5-year renewal option brings the total to 9 years, but that option is not guaranteed -- the landlord may refuse it, or impose dramatically higher rent. Any equipment retooling investment (which is needed for the card system retrofit) must be amortized within the secure lease period. The buyer should attempt to negotiate a lease extension with the landlord BEFORE closing.

**[MODERATE] No security cameras or remote monitoring mentioned.**
For an absentee owner, security cameras with remote access are listed as "Critical" infrastructure. Without cameras, the owner has no visibility into facility condition, customer behavior, vandalism, or attendant performance. Budget $2,000-$5,000 for installation.

**[MINOR] No wash-dry-fold service limits revenue diversification.**
While WDF is not necessary (and adds labor complexity that complicates absentee ownership), the absence of any secondary revenue stream means the business has no buffer if self-service demand softens. This is noted for awareness, not as a recommendation to add WDF.

**[MINOR] Part-time attendant at 20 hours/week -- terms and reliability unknown.**
The attendant is a key operational resource. Their pay rate, tenure, reliability, and willingness to continue under new ownership are unknowns that must be resolved in due diligence.

**6. "Can I see three years of tax returns, including the Schedule C or corporate return?"**
*Non-negotiable. Tax-reported revenue is the only legally defensible revenue number. If the tax returns show $160,000 in revenue while the listing says $210,000, there is a $50,000 gap that is either unreported cash income (buyer cannot finance or value on unreported income) or inflated listing numbers.*
- Good answer: Tax returns provided promptly and align with listed revenue within 5-10%.
- Bad answer: Delay, excuses, or significant discrepancy between tax-reported and claimed revenue.

**7. "What does a typical week look like for the part-time attendant? What exactly do they handle?"**
*Understanding the attendant's actual duties reveals what operational functions would need to be covered under absentee ownership. Does the attendant collect coins? Handle machine jams? Clean bathrooms? Manage the change machine?*
- Good answer: Clear job description with defined duties.
- Bad answer: Vague -- "they just keep an eye on things." (Poorly defined role = operational risk.)

**8. "What's the maintenance arrangement for the machines? Is there a service contract with Speed Queen, Dexter, or a local tech?"**
*Without a formal maintenance contract, the owner is likely doing DIY repairs -- which stops when the owner leaves. For absentee operation, a contracted maintenance tech is essential ($500-$2,000/month). If the owner has been doing all maintenance personally, factor this cost into your SDE adjustment.*
- Good answer: "We have a relationship with [local tech company], and they come out within 24 hours."
- Bad answer: "I handle most repairs myself." (Common for owner-operators, but means the function disappears at close.)

**9. "Has revenue been steady, growing, or declining over the last three years? Any significant changes in the customer base?"**
*The broker said "stable." Stable sometimes means flat, and flat in an inflationary environment means declining in real terms. Three years of monthly revenue data will reveal the trend.*
- Good answer: Revenue has been steady or growing, with data to support it.
- Bad answer: "It's been pretty consistent" with no data. (No data = no answer.)

**10. "How competitive is the immediate area? Are there other laundromats within a 2-3 mile radius, and has anything new opened in the last few years?"**
*A new competitor within 2 miles in a suburban market is a serious threat. Decatur is densely developed enough that competition could be close.*
- Good answer: "There are two others in the area but they're older/smaller. Nothing new has opened."
- Bad answer: "There's a new one that opened last year about a mile away." (This could explain a motivated sale.)

**11. "Are there any current or pending issues with the property -- plumbing, drainage, code violations, ADA compliance?"**
*Laundromats have complex plumbing infrastructure. Older stores may have drainage issues, grease trap problems, or ADA non-compliance that create liability.*
- Good answer: "Everything is up to code, last inspection was [date]."
- Bad answer: Hesitation or "I'd have to check on that."

**12. "If I were to make an offer, would the owner be open to a 90-day transition period where they introduce me to the maintenance tech, the attendant, and walk me through the weekly operations?"**
*Tests the seller's willingness to invest time in a quality handoff. A genuine retiree who cares about the business legacy will agree. An escape artist will push back.*
- Good answer: "Absolutely, the owner wants to make sure the transition is smooth."
- Bad answer: "The owner was hoping for a quick handoff." (Wants out fast -- why?)

## 10. NEGOTIATION POSITION

### BATNA Assessment

**Buyer's BATNA (Alternatives to this deal):**
- The buyer is evaluating multiple deals across janitorial, car wash, and laundromat verticals in the SE US. This is not the only opportunity.
- Laundromat listings in the $250K-$400K range appear regularly in the metro Atlanta and neighboring markets.
- The buyer can wait. Capital held in conservative investments costs opportunity but does not expire.
- **Buyer's BATNA: MODERATE.** The buyer has alternatives, but a genuinely good unattended laundromat at the right price is not common.

**Seller's BATNA (Alternatives to selling to this buyer):**
- The seller is 68 and retiring. Every month of delay costs them personally -- time, energy, continued operational burden.
- The listing is coin-only, which limits the buyer pool. Sophisticated buyers with financing will be deterred by unverifiable revenue. The likely buyer pool is cash buyers or experienced laundromat operators who know how to verify revenue independently.
- Laundromats in the $300K-$400K range are not the hottest segment of the market. Listings can sit for 3-6 months.
- **Seller's BATNA: WEAKENING OVER TIME.** A 68-year-old retiree's negotiating position erodes with every month. Time is on the buyer's side.

### Recommended Opening Offer Range

**Opening offer: $280,000 - $300,000** (27-22% below asking)

Rationale:
- The justified multiple analysis supports a fair price of $288,750-$320,250
- The negative margin of safety at $385,000 means the asking price is not defensible
- A coin-only store with an above-benchmark SDE margin and limited lease term warrants the lower end of the fair range
- An opening offer at $290,000 (24.7% below asking) is within the standard negotiation range for an overpriced deal (25-40% below asking per the negotiation playbook)
- Frame the offer with data: "Based on the coin-only revenue verification discount, the 4-year base lease term, and comparable laundromat transactions in the metro Atlanta market, our analysis supports a value of $290,000."

### Deal Structure Recommendation

**Optimal structure:**

| Component | Amount | Terms |
|---|---|---|
| SBA 7(a) loan | ~$200,000-$210,000 (65-70%) | 10-year term, current SBA rate |
| Seller financing | $60,000-$65,000 (20%) | 6% interest, 7-year term (negotiate down from 7% and extend from 5 years) |
| Buyer equity/down payment | $30,000-$45,000 (10-15%) | Cash at close |
| **Total** | **$290,000-$310,000** | |

**Additional structural elements:**

- **Escrow:** Hold 10% of purchase price ($29,000-$31,000) in escrow for 12 months to cover undisclosed liabilities, revenue shortfalls, or equipment failures.
- **Earnout:** Propose 10% of purchase price ($29,000-$31,000) tied to trailing 12-month revenue meeting or exceeding $200,000. This protects the buyer if the revenue figure was inflated.
- **Transition period:** 90 days minimum. Seller introduces buyer to attendant, maintenance tech, landlord, and walks through coin collection, machine maintenance routines, and vendor relationships.
- **Non-compete:** 5-mile radius, 3-year term. Laundromats are hyper-local; a former owner opening or advising a competitor within 5 miles could divert customers.
- **Lease contingency:** Make the offer contingent on the buyer reviewing and approving the full lease, AND on the landlord confirming in writing that the lease is assignable and the renewal option is in good standing.
- **Card system retrofit:** Negotiate that the seller contribute $10,000-$15,000 toward a card/app system retrofit as a condition of closing, or reduce the price accordingly. Frame it as: "For the business to qualify for SBA financing, we need verifiable revenue systems in place."

### Concession Strategy

Concede in this order (least valuable to buyer first):

1. **Training period details** -- easy to give on exact weeks/hours
2. **Equipment/inventory inclusion** -- standard; not a meaningful concession
3. **Non-compete scope** -- can narrow from 5 miles to 3 miles if needed
4. **Transition period length** -- can accept 60 days if other terms are met
5. **Earnout structure** -- can reduce from 10% to 5% tied to revenue
6. **Seller financing rate** -- can accept 6.5% instead of 6% if seller extends to 7 years
7. **Purchase price** -- fight hardest here; do not exceed $320,000 without verified revenue

**Never give a concession without getting something in return:**
- "We can move to $310,000 IF the seller provides 24 months of utility bills that corroborate the reported revenue within 10%."
- "We'll accept the 5-year seller note term IF the interest rate comes down to 6%."
- "We'll waive the escrow requirement IF the seller agrees to a 12-month earnout tied to revenue."

### Walkaway Price

**Do not exceed $325,000 under any circumstances unless:**
1. Revenue is independently verified through utility cross-referencing AND tax returns, AND
2. The landlord confirms the lease renewal option in writing, AND
3. A detailed equipment inventory confirms the majority of machines are under 7 years old

**If the revenue verification fails** (utility bills do not support $210,000 in revenue), reprice the deal based on verified revenue. If verified revenue is $170,000 with an estimated SDE of $75,000-$85,000, the justified price drops to $200,000-$240,000.

**Absolute walkaway triggers:**
- Seller refuses to provide utility bills or tax returns
- Landlord will not consent to lease assignment
- Equipment inspection reveals average age over 10 years
- Verified revenue is more than 20% below reported ($168,000 or less)
- Lease has a rent escalation clause that would push rent above 25% of revenue within 3 years

## 11. BROKER'S VERDICT
