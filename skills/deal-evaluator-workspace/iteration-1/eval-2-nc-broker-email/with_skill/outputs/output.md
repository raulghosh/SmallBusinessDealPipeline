#### 4. SELLER-SIDE DECODING

**What they are emphasizing and why:**

The broker's email hits five specific points: (1) revenue, (2) SDE margin, (3) recurring revenue, (4) no lease liability, and (5) retirement. Let's decode each:

1. **"$739,926 in annual revenue"** -- Leading with a specific revenue number is standard. The precision suggests this comes from financial statements, which is a mild positive. But revenue is vanity; SDE is sanity.

2. **"32% SDE margin, which is exceptional for this industry"** -- The broker is explicitly framing the margin as exceptional. This is a red flag, not a green flag. When the broker calls attention to the margin being above industry norms, it usually means the add-backs are aggressive and they want to preempt your skepticism by normalizing it before you look closely. A 32% SDE margin in janitorial is in the zone where add-backs should be suspect. The broker knows this and is getting ahead of it.

3. **"True recurring revenue business with a dedicated client base"** -- "Recurring revenue" is the most overused term in cleaning business sales. It is used even when contracts are verbal, month-to-month, and terminable on 30 days notice. "Dedicated client base" is emotional language, not contractual language. Dedicated means nothing if clients can leave tomorrow with zero penalty.

4. **"Home-based, so no lease liability"** -- This is being framed as an advantage. It is partly true (no lease exposure). But it also means: (a) the business has no commercial presence or professional facility, (b) equipment is stored at the owner's home or in the field, raising questions about maintenance and storage, and (c) the buyer would need to establish their own base of operations if they don't want to run a business from their home. For a home-based janitorial company, "no lease" also means the owner is deeply intertwined with operations.

5. **"The owner is retiring after a successful run"** -- "Retiring" is legitimate about 60% of the time. The other 40% masks burnout, client loss, health issues, or market deterioration. The phrase "after a successful run" is an editorial flourish from the broker -- it is not a financial statement. Notice the broker did not say how long the owner has been running the business. This omission is significant.

**What they are likely not telling you:**

- **Year established:** The single most conspicuous omission. If the business has been operating for 15--20 years, the broker would have mentioned it -- longevity is a selling point. The omission suggests the business is either younger than 10 years (weaker), or the founding history is complicated (partnership dissolution, restart, name change).

- **Customer concentration:** Not a word about client diversity. "Dedicated client base" could mean 5 clients who like the owner. Until you see a revenue-by-client breakdown, assume concentration risk is present.

- **Management structure:** With 32 part-time employees and no mention of a GM, operations manager, or even crew leads, the owner is almost certainly the sole manager of this workforce. This is the biggest hidden risk for an absentee buyer.

- **Contract type:** "Recurring revenue" without specifying written contracts likely means verbal agreements and month-to-month arrangements. If the contracts were multi-year and written, the broker would have said so -- it would justify a premium.

- **Employee classification:** 32 part-time employees is a notable structure. Are these W-2 employees or 1099 contractors? Misclassification of 1099s is a common legal liability in janitorial businesses. If any are 1099 and working regular schedules under company direction, the buyer inherits the legal and tax exposure.

- **Seller financing:** Not mentioned. In a retirement sale, the absence of seller financing is a yellow flag. A confident retiree typically offers 10--20% seller financing because (a) they believe the business will perform, and (b) they get tax advantages from an installment sale. Silence on financing may mean the seller wants all cash at close -- which could indicate they don't trust the business to keep generating cash flow, or a lender has already flagged the deal.

- **Revenue trend:** No mention of whether revenue is growing, flat, or declining. If it were growing, they would tell you.

**Add-back red flags:**

No add-back schedule has been provided. The SDE of $239,643 (32% of revenue) is higher than the industry norm of 15--25%. Without a line-by-line add-back breakdown, this number cannot be trusted. Common sources of inflated SDE in home-based janitorial businesses include: spouse on payroll with no real duties, personal vehicle expenses added back, personal cell phone and travel, and depreciation on equipment that actually needs replacing. The buyer must demand the add-back schedule before any further engagement.

**The "retirement" decode:**

Retirement is plausible but unverified. The broker did not mention the owner's age, the number of years in business, or any personal context (health, spouse, next chapter). A genuine retiree who has run a business for 20 years typically lets the broker share those details because they enhance the narrative. The vagueness here suggests either: (a) the owner is younger than typical retirement age and "retiring" is a euphemism, or (b) the business is not as old as a retirement narrative would imply.

2. **32% SDE margin with 32 part-time employees.** Part-time employees are less efficient per revenue dollar than full-time employees (more scheduling complexity, more turnover, more training overhead). #### 9. DUE DILIGENCE PRIORITY QUESTIONS

**1. "Can you share the year the business was established and a bit about its history?"**
Good answer: Specific year, 10+ years, organic growth story. Bad answer: Vague, recent founding, or complicated history involving name changes or restarts.

**2. "I'd love to understand the SDE breakdown -- could you walk me through the major add-backs that get to the $239,643 figure?"**
Good answer: Clean, specific add-backs (owner salary, personal insurance, one-time expense). Bad answer: Vague descriptions, family salary add-backs, recurring "one-time" expenses, or reluctance to provide the schedule.

**3. "What does the client mix look like -- roughly how many active clients, and what does the revenue distribution look like across the top 5?"**
Good answer: 15+ active clients, no single client above 15%. Bad answer: Fewer than 10 clients, or top client is 20%+ of revenue. Evasion is itself the worst answer.

**4. "Who manages the day-to-day operations -- scheduling, dispatch, quality checks? Is there a manager or lead supervisor on the team?"**
Good answer: Named operations manager or lead supervisor with tenure. Bad answer: "The owner handles it but it only takes a few hours a week." This is the most important question for an absentee buyer.

**5. "Are the client relationships under written contracts, and if so, what are the typical terms -- annual, multi-year, month-to-month?"**
Good answer: Written contracts, 1--3 year terms, auto-renewal. Bad answer: "Most are verbal but long-standing." Long-standing verbal agreements are still terminable immediately.

**6. "Is the owner open to seller financing as part of the deal structure? Even a 10--15% seller note would help with alignment."**
Good answer: Yes, willing to carry 10--20% at reasonable terms. Bad answer: All cash only, no exceptions. The response to this question is a direct signal of seller confidence.

**7. "How are the 32 employees classified -- W-2 or 1099? And what has the turnover been like over the past two years?"**
Good answer: All W-2, turnover under 50% annually (low for janitorial). Bad answer: Mix of 1099 and W-2, or turnover above 100%. If any are 1099, request the classification rationale.

**8. "What does the equipment situation look like -- fleet vehicles, floor machines, supplies? Is there an itemized list with approximate ages?"**
Good answer: Detailed list, equipment under 5 years old, maintenance logs. Bad answer: Vague list, "various" ages, replacement-value figures without actual condition data.

**9. "Can you share three years of P&L statements and tax returns so we can see the revenue and margin trend?"**
Good answer: Provided promptly with consistent numbers. Bad answer: Delay, excuses, or significant variance between CIM claims and tax returns.

**10. "What would the owner's ideal transition look like? We'd want to make sure clients and employees are comfortable with the changeover."**
Good answer: 90+ day transition with client introductions and crew meetings. Bad answer: "Two weeks should be plenty" -- this signals either burnout or fear of what extended contact will reveal.

**11. "If we structured part of the price as a 12-month earnout tied to client retention, would the owner be open to that?"**
Good answer: Open to discussing it. A genuine retiree with a healthy business has no reason to refuse. Bad answer: Hard refusal -- suggests the owner expects client attrition post-sale.
