## 4. SELLER-SIDE DECODING

### What They Are Emphasizing and Why

**"Tremendous growth potential -- this wash is only scratching the surface of what this location can do."**
This is textbook broker language for: *current performance does not justify the asking price.* The broker is asking the buyer to pay for hypothetical future revenue, not present cash flow. When a $1.2M revenue business with $420K SDE is priced at 4.4x, the multiple already implies growth expectations. The broker's language confirms that the seller knows the current numbers do not support $1,850,000 without a growth narrative.

**Decode:** If the location had "tremendous growth potential," why is the seller leaving after only 9 years? An express tunnel in a 45,000-car-per-day location in a growing Atlanta suburb should be accelerating, not changing hands. The seller's departure contradicts the broker's growth pitch.

**"Perfect for an investor looking for passive income."**
This is the absentee hook. It is true that express tunnel car washes are among the best absentee car wash models, but "perfect for passive income" is a marketing phrase, not a verified operational fact. It needs to be validated with evidence of a site manager on staff, documented SOPs, and a POS system with remote monitoring capability.

**"800+ active members."**
The word "active" is doing heavy lifting here. Key questions:
- **What does "active" mean?** Billed in the last 30 days? Or ever signed up? Sellers are known to count everyone who ever signed up, not just currently paying members.
- **What is the monthly churn rate?** If churn is 8%+ per month, the membership base is a revolving door, not a sticky asset. At 800 members with 8% churn, 64 members leave every month and must be replaced just to stay flat.
- **What percentage of the $1.2M revenue comes from memberships?** If 800 members pay an average of $40/month, that is $384,000/year, or 32% of revenue. That is below the "good" threshold of 35%+ and well below the "excellent" threshold of 55%+. This means the majority of revenue is transactional (single washes) and weather-dependent.
- **What is the membership trend?** Growing, flat, or declining over the last 24 months? A "growing" base that has been at ~800 for two years is not growing.

**"Fully automated tunnel system installed 2019, water reclamation system."**
This is good equipment to have, but 2019 installation means the tunnel is now 7 years old. Express tunnel systems have a 10-15 year lifespan with a major rebuild recommended at 7-10 years. The equipment is entering the zone where significant maintenance and component replacement costs begin to escalate. The $850,000 FF&E figure likely represents replacement cost, not current value. A 7-year-old tunnel system is worth considerably less than $850K -- perhaps $400K-$550K in current condition, depending on maintenance history.

**"45,000+ vehicles per day on adjacent road."**
Traffic count claims should always be verified with state DOT data, not the seller's estimate. 45,000 is a good number (above the 25,000 minimum, near the 40,000+ ideal for express tunnels), but the word "adjacent" matters. Is the site directly on the high-traffic road with excellent visibility and ingress/egress? Or is it on a side street "adjacent" to the main road, requiring a turn? The difference in capture rate is enormous.

### What They Are Likely Not Telling You

1. **Membership churn rate and trend.** If the membership base were growing at a strong clip with low churn, the broker would lead with those numbers. The vague "800+ active members" without churn data is a deflection signal.
2. **Monthly and seasonal revenue breakdown.** Car wash revenue in the SE US swings 30-50% between peak (spring pollen season, Q2) and trough (summer hurricane/thunderstorm season, Q3). The $1.2M annual figure hides this volatility. The SDE could vary dramatically by quarter.
3. **Equipment maintenance history and condition.** A 7-year-old tunnel that has been well-maintained is very different from one that has been run hard without proper service. No maintenance logs have been offered or referenced.
4. **Whether the owner is the site manager.** If the owner is currently filling the site manager role, the SDE is overstated by $50K+ because a replacement manager salary is not being deducted from operating costs. This is the most common SDE inflation method in owner-operated car washes.
5. **Competitive landscape.** What PE-backed chains are operating within 3 miles? Is a new Tidal Wave or Zips location under construction in the Kennesaw area? Atlanta suburbs are ground zero for car wash consolidation.

### Add-Back Red Flags

No add-back schedule has been provided. The $420,000 SDE figure is unverified and should be treated as aspirational until the buyer receives:
- 3 years of tax returns

At 35% SDE margin, the number is within the plausible range for express tunnel (25-40%), but is on the higher side. This could indicate efficient operations or it could indicate deferred maintenance, understaffing, or aggressive add-backs. Verification is mandatory.

### The "Pursuing Other Business Ventures" Decode

This is not a retirement sale. The seller is not 65 years old cashing out after a career. "Pursuing other business ventures" from a business established in 2017 (9 years ago) has several possible translations:
- The seller built or bought this wash as an investment, captured the appreciation, and wants to exit at a peak multiple before equipment replacement costs hit.
- The seller is a serial entrepreneur who is bored or distracted.
- The business has hit a ceiling the seller cannot break through (flat membership growth, competition encroaching).
- The seller sees something coming (new competitor, lease issue, regulatory change, market saturation) that will make the next 3-5 years less profitable than the last 3-5.

The critical question is: **Why now?** If the location truly has "tremendous growth potential," a rational seller would stay and capture that growth themselves. The decision to sell a high-potential asset contradicts the growth pitch -- unless the seller knows the growth story is more aspirational than factual.

## 5. SELLER'S AXE

### Seller Archetype: **Likely Burnout or Strategic Exiter -- NOT a Genuine Retiree**

This seller does not fit the Genuine Retiree profile. The business is only 9 years old. The reason for selling is "pursuing other business ventures," not retirement or health. The listing does not mention the seller's age, length of involvement, or emotional attachment to the business -- all hallmarks of a genuine retiree who is proud of what they built.

The most likely archetypes are:

**Scenario A -- Strategic Exiter (variant of Burnout):** The seller built or bought this wash as an investment, ran it through the growth phase (2017-2023), and now wants to exit before the capital-intensive maintenance phase begins. The tunnel is 7 years old. In 3-5 years, major rebuild costs ($500K-$1.5M for a full tunnel system) will hit. By selling now, the seller captures the premium of a "working tunnel with growing membership" without bearing the cost of replacing or rebuilding the tunnel. This is a sophisticated move by a seller who understands equipment lifecycle economics.

**Scenario B -- The Escape Artist (lower probability but possible):** The seller sees a threat the buyer cannot yet see. Possibilities include: a PE-backed chain (Tidal Wave, Zips) has announced or is constructing a new location within the competitive radius; membership growth has stalled or reversed; a key operational issue (equipment, environmental permit, lease) is looming.

### The Real Reason for Selling

Most probable: **The seller has done the math on upcoming equipment replacement CapEx and decided that selling at a 4.4x multiple now is more profitable than spending $500K-$1.5M on a tunnel rebuild in 3-5 years and selling later.** This is the classic car wash "sell before the capex cliff" play.

### Consistency Check

- The "tremendous growth potential" narrative conflicts with the seller's decision to sell. If growth is truly tremendous, why leave?
- The $420,000 SDE is healthy, but the 4.4x multiple prices in significant future performance that the seller apparently does not believe enough to stay for.
- No seller financing is mentioned. If the seller truly believed in the business's future cash flow, carrying 10-15% of the purchase price in a seller note would be an easy tax-advantaged move. The absence of seller financing from a non-retirement seller is a yellow flag.
- The equipment was installed in 2019 (7 years ago). At the 10-15 year tunnel lifespan, the seller is exiting at the midpoint of equipment life -- right before maintenance costs begin to accelerate.

### What to Watch For

- **Ask the seller directly: "What would you do with this wash if you kept it for 5 more years?"** A strategic exiter will give a vague answer or deflect. A genuine seller will have specific growth plans they simply don't have time for.
- **Ask: "If we offered you a 3-year earnout tied to revenue retention, would that be interesting?"** A seller who knows the business will perform post-sale will be open to this. A seller who is exiting before problems emerge will push for all cash at close.
- **Ask: "Has anything changed competitively in this market in the last 12 months?"** Watch for body language and specificity. Vague answers like "the market is healthy" are deflections.
- **Watch response timing.** If the seller responds same-day or pushes for a fast LOI, urgency is high. Why? The longer a car wash listing sits, the more potential buyers drive the site and see what the seller doesn't want them to see.

## 9. DUE DILIGENCE PRIORITY QUESTIONS

**1. "Can you share the membership data for the last 24 months -- specifically, active members by month, new sign-ups, cancellations, and monthly churn rate?"**
*Good answer:* Churn below 5%/month, membership trending upward, data provided promptly from POS system. *Bad answer:* Vague answer ("we don't track it that way"), churn above 8%/month, membership has been flat or declining, or data is delayed more than one week.

**2. "What percentage of the $1.2M revenue comes from memberships vs. retail single washes vs. fleet accounts vs. other sources?"**
*Good answer:* 35%+ from memberships, detailed breakdown provided, fleet accounts diversified. *Bad answer:* Less than 25% from memberships (business is primarily transactional and weather-dependent), or broker deflects with "we can provide that under NDA." Revenue segmentation is not an NDA-gated item.

**3. "Is there a dedicated site manager currently on staff? If so, what is their name, tenure, salary, and scope of authority?"**
*Good answer:* Named site manager with 1+ year tenure, $45K-$55K salary, authority to hire/fire hourly staff and make equipment repair decisions. *Bad answer:* "The owner handles most of that" or "the lead employee covers when the owner is not there." If the owner is the site manager, the SDE is overstated and the absentee model is unproven.

**4. "Can you provide the equipment maintenance logs for the tunnel system since its 2019 installation? Specifically, conveyor chain measurements, parts replacement history, and any major rebuild work?"**
*Good answer:* Detailed maintenance records provided, regular service intervals documented, chain within manufacturer spec. *Bad answer:* "The equipment is in great shape" without documentation. No maintenance logs is a red flag indicating deferred maintenance.

**5. "The listing mentions 45,000+ vehicles per day. Can you share the source for that traffic count -- specifically, the Georgia DOT study or traffic report?"**
*Good answer:* Specific DOT report or study referenced, ideally within the last 2-3 years. *Bad answer:* "That's what the seller told us" or "it's based on our estimate." Unverified traffic counts are marketing, not data.

**6. "What is the lease situation? If the buyer does not purchase the real estate, what are the lease terms -- length, renewal options, rent amount, escalation clauses, and assignment rights?"**
*Good answer:* 10+ year lease with renewal options, rent escalation capped at 2-3%/year, fully assignable. *Bad answer:* Short-term lease (<5 years), steep escalation clauses, or seller is vague about lease terms. For a car wash, the lease IS the business.

**7. "Can you share 36 months of monthly revenue data from the POS system, broken out by wash type and membership revenue?"**
*Good answer:* Data provided promptly showing seasonal patterns and year-over-year trends. *Bad answer:* Only annual figures available, or data is limited to trailing 12 months. The buyer needs to see seasonal volatility and membership revenue trends.

**8. "Why is the owner selling now, specifically? The business was established in 2017 and the broker describes it as having tremendous growth potential -- what changed?"**
*Good answer:* Consistent, specific answer (e.g., "I'm starting a restaurant" or "I want to invest in a different market"). *Bad answer:* Vague answer that shifts across conversations, over-explanation ("the business is great, I just..."), or deflection to the broker. Per the three-conversation test, ask this question again in different forms during a site visit and during due diligence.

**9. "What is the competitive landscape within a 3-mile radius? Are there any new car washes under construction or announced in the Kennesaw area?"**
*Good answer:* Honest inventory of competitors with specific names and distances. *Bad answer:* "There's no real competition" or evasion. In a metro Atlanta suburb, there is always competition.

**10. "Would the seller be open to seller financing -- say, 15% of the purchase price as a seller note over 5-7 years?"**
*Good answer:* "Yes, the seller would consider it." This signals confidence in future performance. *Bad answer:* "No, the seller wants all cash at close." From a non-retirement seller, cash-only is a signal that the seller does not want their money tied to this business's future.

**11. "What are the annual insurance costs, including general liability, property (if RE is acquired), workers' comp, and flood/wind? Have there been any claims in the past 5 years?"**
*Good answer:* Detailed breakdown, reasonable costs, clean claims history. *Bad answer:* Costs are unusually high (suggesting prior claims), or claims history is not disclosed.

**12. "Can we verify the environmental permits -- wastewater discharge, stormwater, and chemical storage -- and confirm they are current and transferable? Any Notices of Violation in the last 5 years?"**
*Good answer:* Permits are current, transferable, and NOV-free. *Bad answer:* Permits are expired, non-transferable, or there are outstanding NOVs. Environmental compliance issues can cost $10K-$50K+ to remediate.

## 10. NEGOTIATION POSITION

**Buyer's BATNA:** Moderate. The buyer is evaluating deals across janitorial, car wash, and laundromat verticals in multiple SE US states. This is not the only deal in the pipeline. The buyer can walk and continue sourcing. Express tunnel car washes in the $1M-$2M range do come to market regularly in metro Atlanta. Time is on the buyer's side.

**Seller's BATNA:** Moderate to Weakening. The seller is "pursuing other business ventures," which implies they want capital freed up. Every month the business sits unsold, the tunnel equipment gets one month older and closer to the rebuild cliff. If the listing has been active for 60+ days, the seller's BATNA is weakening. If it has been active for 90+, the seller is approaching motivated territory. The absence of mentioned seller financing suggests the seller either expects full-price offers or has been coached to demand all-cash -- both of which can be negotiated once the deal stalls.

**Leverage assessment:** The buyer has moderate leverage. The car wash industry is in a consolidation wave, and there are more buyers than quality assets. However, the specific pricing of this deal at 4.4x SDE (at the ceiling) gives the buyer room to argue for a meaningful price reduction using data-based justification.

### Recommended Opening Offer Range

**$1,350,000 - $1,450,000 for the business only (27-22% below asking)**

Justification:
- The justified multiple based on the valuation adjustment worksheet is approximately 3.65x SDE = $1,533,000
- Opening below justified value gives room to negotiate upward toward justified value while still landing below asking
- At $1,400,000, the SDE multiple is 3.33x -- solidly mid-range for express/tunnel and a fair price for a 9-year-old business with 7-year-old equipment and an unverified membership base
- Frame the offer with data: "Based on comparable express tunnel transactions in the SE US, current equipment condition, and the need to budget for tunnel equipment rebuild within 3-5 years, we've arrived at..."

### Deal Structure Recommendation

**Optimal structure:**
- Purchase price: $1,400,000 (business only)
- SBA 7(a) financing: 75% ($1,050,000)
- Buyer down payment: 10% ($140,000)
- Seller note: 15% ($210,000) at 6% interest, 7-year term
- Transition period: 90 days with seller as paid consultant, tied to operational handoff milestones
- Earnout: 10% of purchase price ($140,000) tied to 12-month revenue retention and membership retention benchmarks
- Escrow: 7.5% ($105,000) held for 12 months against undisclosed liabilities
- Non-compete: 25-mile radius, 5 years, car wash and related services

**On the real estate:** If the buyer also acquires the RE, negotiate separately. The $2.1M asking price should be independently appraised. A cap rate approach using market rent for the car wash site would provide a more accurate RE valuation. Consider acquiring the RE through a separate LLC to isolate real estate risk from operating risk -- this structure is also preferable for H1B compliance. Total RE + business should not exceed $3.2M-$3.4M.

### Concession Strategy

Give these up in order (easiest first):
1. **Training period extension** (low cost -- offer 4 weeks of post-close phone availability)
2. **Equipment/inventory inclusion** (standard -- already included in asking)
3. **Earnout reduction** (reduce from 10% to 5% if seller pushes back, but do not eliminate)
4. **Transition period reduction** (drop from 90 to 60 days if needed)
5. **Price increase** (move from $1,400K to $1,500K maximum, but only if seller concedes on seller financing and earnout)
6. **Seller note terms** (adjust rate from 6% to 7% or term from 7 to 5 years -- last resort)

**Never concede on:** Escrow (non-negotiable for a non-retirement seller), the right to verify all financials with tax returns and POS data, and the right to a Phase I ESA on the real estate.

### Walkaway Price

**$1,600,000 for the business only.** Above this price (3.81x SDE), the deal does not generate sufficient return for the risk profile. At $1,600,000:
- SDE multiple: 3.81x (within range but above midpoint)
- With SBA financing of $1,200,000 at 10.5% over 10 years: annual debt service of ~$192,000
- Owner's Earnings of $264,000 / $192,000 = DSCR of 1.375x (tight but acceptable)
- No margin of safety from intrinsic value perspective

Above $1,600,000, the debt service coverage becomes dangerously thin and the buyer is paying for growth that has not been demonstrated.

**Total deal walkaway (business + RE):** $3,400,000 combined. Above this, the capital allocation does not justify the risk.

## 11. BROKER'S VERDICT

**PURSUE WITH CONDITIONS**

This is a fundamentally sound business model (express tunnel with membership) in a strong location (growing Atlanta suburb, high traffic counts) -- but it is overpriced at $1,850,000 and has several unresolved questions that must be answered before any offer. The 4.4x SDE multiple is at the ceiling of the range for a business that has not demonstrated ceiling-quality characteristics: the membership base is below 1,000, the equipment is entering its rebuild window, the seller is not retiring, and no seller financing is offered.

**Conditions for pursuit:**
1. Verify the membership data: active count, churn rate, revenue percentage, and 24-month trend. If churn is above 8%/month or membership revenue is below 25% of total, the valuation must be reduced significantly.
2. Confirm the existence and tenure of a dedicated site manager. If the owner is the site manager, the SDE is overstated and the absentee model is unproven.
3. Obtain and independently assess tunnel equipment maintenance logs. If no logs exist, budget for accelerated replacement CapEx and reduce the offer accordingly.
4. Verify the 45,000+ traffic count with Georgia DOT data.
5. Clarify the real estate lease terms if the buyer does not acquire the RE. If no long-term lease (10+ years with renewal options) is available, the business value is severely discounted.

**The single biggest risk the buyer must resolve before going under contract:** The tunnel equipment is 7 years old and approaching the major rebuild window (7-10 years). A full tunnel system replacement costs $500K-$1.5M. If maintenance logs reveal deferred maintenance, the true cost of ownership over the next 5 years could erase the investment returns entirely. The buyer must see maintenance records and ideally commission an independent equipment inspection before making an offer.

**What would make this a PURSUE NOW:** Asking price at or below $1,500,000; verified membership base above 1,000 with sub-5% monthly churn contributing 40%+ of revenue; confirmed site manager with 2+ years tenure; tunnel equipment independently inspected with 5+ years of remaining useful life confirmed; and seller willing to carry 15%+ as a seller note.
