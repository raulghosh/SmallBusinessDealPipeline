#### 4. SELLER-SIDE DECODING

**What they are emphasizing and why:**

The listing title leads with the revenue number -- "$1.3M Multi-Service Cleaning & Property Maintenance Company." Per the seller playbook, leading with revenue hides thin margins. And indeed, the SDE margin is 16.9%, which is on the lower end for janitorial. They want you focused on the impressive topline, not the modest bottom line.

The broker describes it as "a staple in the community for over two decades." This is the longevity pitch -- and in this case, 24 years is genuinely impressive. But note what comes next: "ready for a new owner to take to the next level." Per the seller playbook, this phrase translates directly to: **growth has stalled and the seller has given up trying.** The current owner has hit their ceiling, and the business is being positioned as a "growth opportunity" rather than a currently growing enterprise.

The multi-service angle (cleaning + property maintenance + landscaping support) is likely being emphasized to justify the revenue figure. But multi-service also means multi-complexity -- more service lines mean more operational oversight, more employee skill requirements, and more client touchpoints. For an absentee H1B buyer, this is a complication, not a feature.

**What they are likely not telling you:**

1. **Customer concentration:** At $1.3M revenue across commercial and residential cleaning plus property maintenance, there are likely a few anchor commercial accounts that drive 30--50% of revenue. The listing does not break down revenue by client, by service line, or by contract type. This is the single most important missing piece of information.

2. **Owner-dependency:** A business founded in 2002 and run for 24 years by the same owner almost certainly runs on that owner's personal relationships. The owner likely handles client complaints, does the bidding on new contracts, manages crew scheduling, and maintains the key commercial accounts through personal rapport. The absence of any mention of a GM, operations manager, or management team is deafening.

3. **Employee count and turnover:** Employees are "not disclosed." For a $1.3M multi-service cleaning company, you would expect 17--25+ employees (using the $50K--$80K revenue per FTE benchmark for janitorial). The refusal to disclose headcount suggests either high turnover, workforce classification issues (1099 misclassification), or a reliance on subcontractors that makes the business structurally fragile.

4. **Contract quality:** No mention of written contracts, contract terms, or recurring revenue percentage. The words "recurring revenue" do not appear in the listing. For a cleaning business, this is a critical omission. The revenue may rest on verbal month-to-month agreements that could walk with zero notice.

5. **Equipment and vehicle condition:** FF&E is listed at $40,000 "included." For a $1.3M multi-service operation with cleaning, property maintenance, and landscaping, $40,000 in equipment is surprisingly low. This suggests either the equipment is old and nearly fully depreciated, or there is additional equipment not being disclosed (owner's personal vehicles used for business, for example). Alternatively, the business relies heavily on labor rather than equipment, which is consistent with lower margins.

**Add-back red flags:**

The listing provides an SDE of $227,898 but does not provide an add-back schedule. Without seeing a line-by-line breakdown of what was added back to arrive at SDE, this number is unverified and should be treated as aspirational until confirmed by tax returns and P&L statements. At 16.9% of revenue, the SDE is plausible, but plausible is not verified.

**The "retirement" exit reason -- decoded honestly:**

The stated reason is retirement. The business was established in 2002. If the owner was 35--40 at founding, they are now 59--64 -- a plausible retirement age. The retirement story is credible on the surface.

However, the combination of "retirement" + "ready for a new owner to take to the next level" suggests this is a burnout-adjacent retirement. The owner has likely been running this business for 24 years, hit a revenue and growth ceiling, and is physically and mentally done. They are not fleeing from a specific problem; they are simply finished. This is the most common genuine seller profile in 20+ year janitorial businesses.

The question is whether "done" means they have maintained the business well through their last years, or whether they have been coasting and deferring maintenance, client development, and employee investment for the last 2--3 years. The answer to that question is in the trailing 3-year financials, which you do not yet have.

#### 9. DUE DILIGENCE PRIORITY QUESTIONS

**1. "Can you walk me through the revenue breakdown by service line -- what percentage comes from commercial cleaning, residential cleaning, property maintenance, and landscaping?"**
- Good answer: Specific percentages with commercial cleaning as 60%+ of revenue.

**2. "How many W-2 employees does the business currently have, and how many W-2s were issued in the last 12 months?"**
- Good answer: Current headcount of 18--25 with W-2 count within 30% of that number (normal cleaning industry turnover).
- Bad answer: Evasion, or W-2 count is 2--3x the current headcount (extreme turnover), or heavy reliance on 1099 subcontractors (misclassification risk and legal liability).

**3. "Is there an operations manager, GM, or lead supervisor currently on payroll who handles day-to-day scheduling and client communication?"**
- Good answer: "Yes, [Name] has been with us for [X] years and handles [specific duties]."
- Bad answer: "I handle most of that myself, but the crew leads are very reliable." This confirms owner-dependency and scores the absentee viability at the bottom of the range.

**4. "Could you share the top 10 clients by annual revenue, along with the contract type for each -- whether it's a multi-year written contract, annual, or month-to-month?"**
- Good answer: Diversified client base with no single client above 15% of revenue, and at least 50% of revenue under written contracts.
- Bad answer: Any single client above 20% of revenue. Any indication that most revenue is on verbal or month-to-month agreements. Broker deflection with "we'll share that after LOI."

**5. "Can you clarify the real estate situation -- is the property included in the $649,000 asking price, or would I be leasing the space from you post-close? If leasing, at what terms?"**
- Good answer: Real estate is included at fair value, or a below-market long-term lease is offered.
- Bad answer: Real estate is NOT included and the seller plans to charge above-market rent. This is a classic trap where the seller profits twice -- on the sale and on the lease.

**6. "Could I see the trailing three years of P&L statements and the two most recent tax returns?"**
- Good answer: Provided within one week of NDA signing, with revenue, expenses, and SDE that match the listing within 5%.
- Bad answer: Delay beyond 2 weeks, or material discrepancies between listed SDE and tax-reported income.

**7. "You mentioned retirement -- how long have you been planning this, and what does your ideal transition look like?"**
- Good answer: "I've been thinking about this for a couple of years. I'd be happy to stay on for 3--6 months and introduce you to every client."
- Bad answer: "I'd like to wrap this up quickly" or a transition offer under 30 days. This signals either burnout or something they want to exit before you discover.

**8. "If you were keeping the business, what would you do differently in the next 12 months?"**
- This is a trigger question from the behavioral reads framework. Genuine retirees answer this honestly and reveal unaddressed opportunities. Escape artists reveal the problems they did not fix because they are leaving. Listen carefully to what they say -- it will tell you where the business is weak.

**9. "Would you be open to structuring part of the purchase price as an earnout tied to client retention over 12 months?"**
- Good answer: "I'd consider that -- I'm confident the clients will stay."
- Bad answer: Recoil, resistance, or "I need all the money at close." Per the behavioral reads, Escape Artists refuse earnouts because they know revenue will decline. Genuine Retirees are typically open to it.

**10. "Can I get an itemized equipment list with year purchased, current condition, and mileage for any vehicles?"**
- Good answer: Detailed list provided promptly.
- Bad answer: Single-line "various equipment, $40,000." This confirms the FF&E is aging and not well-tracked.

**11. "How are new clients typically acquired? Is there a sales pipeline, or does most new business come through the owner's network and referrals?"**
- Good answer: Documented sales process, Google presence, referral from existing clients through company reputation.
- Bad answer: "Most of our business comes through my relationships." This means the sales engine leaves with the owner.

**12. "Are there any pending legal matters, regulatory actions, or unresolved disputes I should be aware of?"**
- Good answer: "No, we're clean."
- Bad answer: Hesitation, or "nothing material." In acquisition language, "nothing material" means "there is something, but I've been advised I don't have to tell you yet."

#### 10. NEGOTIATION POSITION

**BATNA Assessment:**

*Buyer's BATNA:* Moderate. The buyer is an H1B visa holder seeking an absentee-operable business. The pool of genuinely absentee-suitable janitorial businesses in the SE US at this price point is limited. However, the buyer should not signal this constraint -- it would weaken their position. The buyer's real BATNA is: (a) other deals currently on the market in the Nashville metro area, (b) waiting for a better deal to emerge, and (c) investing the capital elsewhere. The buyer should have 2--3 other deals in evaluation before making any offer.

*Seller's BATNA:* Moderate-to-Weakening. The seller is a retiree who has been running this business for 24 years. Every month of delay costs them -- personally, emotionally, and in opportunity cost of delayed retirement. The listing is through Transworld Business Advisors, a reputable firm, which suggests the seller is serious and properly advised. However, we do not know how long the listing has been active. If it has been listed for 90+ days, the seller's BATNA is weakening. If less than 30 days, the seller still has time and leverage.

*Who has more leverage:* Approximately balanced, with time favoring the buyer. The seller needs to sell (retirement clock is ticking). The buyer needs to find an absentee-suitable deal (H1B constraint), but the buyer has the luxury of waiting and the seller does not.

**Recommended Opening Offer Range: $475,000 -- $525,000**

Rationale: The justified multiple is 2.55x SDE = ~$581,000. An opening offer of $500,000 represents 2.19x SDE -- a 23% discount from asking, which is appropriate for an overpriced-but-not-egregiously-so deal with significant unresolved questions (absentee viability, client concentration, management depth). This anchors the negotiation in the buyer's territory while leaving room to move toward $550,000--$575,000 if due diligence is favorable.
