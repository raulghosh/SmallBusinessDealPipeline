# Behavioral Reads — Seller & Broker Intelligence
## Reading People in Small Business Acquisitions: Janitorial, Car Wash, Laundromat

---

## SELLER MOTIVATION DECODER

Every seller has a stated reason for selling and a real reason. They are sometimes the same.
Your job is to figure out which archetype you're dealing with, because the archetype determines
your strategy, your risk, and your leverage.

### Archetype 1: The Genuine Retiree

**Profile:** Owner is 55-70+, has run the business 10-20+ years, is financially comfortable,
and is ready to move on. This is the ideal seller.

| Behavioral Signal | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Consistent story across multiple conversations | Not hiding anything — the narrative doesn't shift |
| Willing to do a 90-180 day transition | Wants the business to succeed after them; not running from anything |
| Proactively offers seller financing (10-20% of price) | Confident the business will perform; wants the tax advantage of installment sale |
| Not in a rush — "I'll sell when the right buyer comes along" | Strong BATNA; patient sellers are honest sellers |
| Business listed at fair multiple (2.5-3.5x SDE for cleaning) | Realistic expectations, not fishing |
| Happy to answer operational questions in detail | Nothing to hide; proud of what they built |
| Can name every major client, key employee, and their tenure | Deeply involved, knows the business cold |

**Buyer strategy:** Pay a fair price. Don't try to steal the deal — you'll lose it. Focus on
transition quality and relationship preservation. The genuine retiree's goodwill during
transition is worth 10-15% of deal value in retained clients.

### Archetype 2: The Burnout

**Profile:** Owner is 35-55, has run the business 3-10 years, is exhausted, and wants their
life back. May price aggressively low (just wants out) or irrationally high (anchored to
what they "deserve" for their suffering).

| Behavioral Signal | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Wants a short transition (30 days or less) | Cannot stomach one more day; will not invest in a quality handoff |
| Deferred maintenance visible (dirty vehicles, worn equipment, messy office) | Stopped investing in the business months ago |
| Employee turnover is elevated (people sense the owner's disengagement) | The team is already fraying |
| Recent price drops on the listing | Initially overpriced (emotional anchor), now adjusting to reality |
| Vague on growth plans — "I just don't have the energy" | Honest, but reveals the business has been coasting |
| Financials show flat or declining revenue over last 12-24 months | Business is in mild decay, not crisis |

**Buyer strategy:** This is a value opportunity if the business fundamentals are sound.
The burnout hasn't broken the business — they've just stopped growing it. Key risk: the
transition will be poor. Mitigate by hiring a GM before close, negotiating a longer transition
with financial incentives (final seller note payments tied to transition milestones), and
having the GM shadow the owner, not you.

### Archetype 3: The Escape Artist

**Profile:** Owner is hiding a material problem — client loss, regulatory action, partnership
blowup, health crisis, or financial distress. They need to sell before the problem becomes
visible. This is the most dangerous seller.

| Behavioral Signal | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Evasive on specific revenue questions — gives ranges, not numbers | Doesn't want you to pin down the actual performance |
| Rushed timeline — "I need to close within 60 days" | Problem is on a clock (client leaving, lease expiring, legal deadline) |
| Won't offer seller financing under any terms | Knows the business won't perform well enough to service the note |
| Inconsistent numbers — CIM says one thing, tax returns say another | Financials have been dressed up |
| Gets defensive or emotional when asked normal due diligence questions | Hiding something and feels the pressure |
| Won't introduce you to key employees or clients pre-close | Afraid of what they'll say |
| Story changes: first "retirement," then "want to pursue other opportunities," then "health reasons" | The real reason keeps leaking through different cover stories |

**Buyer strategy:** Do not buy unless you can verify every material fact independently. If
the seller won't cooperate with due diligence, walk immediately. If you do proceed: escrow
20% of price for 12 months, require full reps and warranties with personal indemnification,
and verify every client contract directly with the client (not through the seller).

### Archetype 4: The Fisher

**Profile:** Owner is "testing the market" but not committed to selling. May be gathering
data for a valuation, checking if they can retire earlier than planned, or ego-testing
what the market says their business is worth.

| Behavioral Signal | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Overpriced by 30%+ relative to comparables | Not serious about transacting at market |
| Slow to respond (5+ business days per email) | Low urgency, low commitment |
| Won't share financials even under NDA | Not ready for real scrutiny |
| "Just seeing what it's worth" or "exploring options" | Telling you directly that they're not serious |
| Keeps rescheduling calls or site visits | Not prioritizing the sale process |
| Broker admits the seller is "early in the process" | Broker is managing your expectations (good broker) |

**Buyer strategy:** Do not invest significant time or money in due diligence. Express interest,
state your criteria clearly, and tell the broker: "When the seller is ready to move forward at
a market-based price, we'd be happy to reengage." Move on. The fisher sometimes becomes a
genuine seller 6-12 months later — stay in the broker's pipeline.

## DEFLECTION PATTERN CATALOG

When sellers or brokers avoid direct answers, the evasion itself is information. Here is a
catalog of common deflections and their likely translations.

| What They Say | What It Usually Means | Severity |
|---|---|---|
| "We can discuss that under NDA" (for basic info like revenue range, employee count, years in business) | Hiding something basic that would screen you out. Legitimate sellers share high-level info freely. | HIGH — may not be worth signing the NDA |
| "Revenue has been stable" (without providing trend data) | Revenue is declining. "Stable" is the word people use when they can't say "growing." | MODERATE — demand the actuals |
| "The team is solid" (without naming anyone or their tenure) | High turnover, or the "team" is owner + 2 temps. | MODERATE — request an org chart with hire dates |
| "I'm involved but it mostly runs itself" | Owner IS the business. "Mostly" means 30-40 hours/week of owner time minimum. | HIGH — test with the absentee scoring rubric |
| "We haven't needed formal contracts" | Revenue is on month-to-month verbal agreements. Every dollar is at risk of walking with 0 days notice. | CRITICAL — value the business as if 50% of revenue could leave in 90 days |
| "The financials are with my accountant" (for > 2 weeks) | Financials are being cleaned up, or they don't want you to see the real ones. Accountants don't take 2 weeks to pull tax returns. | HIGH — set a deadline: "We need financials by [date] or we'll move to other opportunities" |
| "There's a lot of upside potential" | Current performance doesn't justify the price. Seller is asking you to pay for hypothetical growth. | MODERATE — ignore the "potential," value on actuals |
| "We've had a lot of interest" | Standard broker pressure tactic. Usually means: no one has made a serious offer. | LOW — ignore and proceed at your pace |
| "The owner is flexible on terms" | Price is too high and they know it. "Flexible" means they'll make it work through creative structure. | LOW — good for you, use it |
| "Some of the revenue is cash" (for laundromat/car wash) | Revenue is underreported on taxes. You can't verify it, you can't finance it, and you can't value on it. | CRITICAL — value only on documented, tax-reported revenue |

## CONSISTENCY TESTING FRAMEWORK

Trust nothing. Verify everything. Cross-reference every material claim against independent data.

### Revenue Verification

| Data Source | What to Cross-Reference | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|
| Tax returns (3 years) | Does reported revenue match the CIM/listing? | Variance > 10% between claimed and reported |
| Bank statements (24 months) | Do deposits match reported revenue? | Deposits consistently lower than claimed revenue |
| Utility bills (laundromat-specific) | Water/gas usage should correlate with machine cycles and revenue | Low utility bills + high claimed revenue = inflated numbers |
| Payroll records | Headcount should match revenue capacity | Too few employees for claimed revenue (who is doing the work?) |
| Credit card processing statements | For car wash / laundromat: % of revenue through card readers | Large gap between card revenue and total claimed revenue (unexplained "cash") |

### Employee-to-Revenue Sanity Check

| Vertical | Revenue per Full-Time Employee | Revenue per Part-Time Employee |
|---|---|---|
| Janitorial / Commercial Cleaning | $50,000-$80,000 | $20,000-$35,000 |
| Car Wash (express/tunnel) | $80,000-$150,000 | $40,000-$60,000 |
| Laundromat (attended) | $100,000-$200,000+ | $50,000-$80,000 |
| Laundromat (unattended) | N/A — minimal labor | N/A |

If claimed revenue is $800K for a janitorial company with 6 full-time employees, the math
says capacity is $300K-$480K. Where is the other $320K-$500K coming from? Either there are
undisclosed employees (subcontractors?), the revenue is inflated, or the employees are
severely underpaid and overworked (turnover risk).

### SDE Margin Sanity Check

| Vertical | Typical SDE Margin (% of Revenue) | Suspicious If |
|---|---|---|
| Janitorial / Commercial Cleaning | 15-25% | SDE margin > 30% (likely underreporting labor or expenses) or < 12% (something is wrong operationally) |
| Car Wash | 25-40% | SDE margin > 45% (deferring maintenance) or < 20% (inefficient operations or high lease cost) |
| Laundromat | 30-45% | SDE margin > 50% (underreporting lease cost or not maintaining machines) or < 25% (bad lease economics) |

### The "Suspiciously Round Numbers" Rule

If the seller reports:
- Revenue: $500,000
- SDE: $150,000
- Margin: exactly 30%

These are almost certainly estimates, not actuals. Real businesses have numbers like $487,231
in revenue and $138,412 in SDE. Round numbers mean the seller (or broker) is approximating
rather than reporting from financial statements. **Demand the underlying data:** tax returns,
P&L statements, bank statements. If they can't produce them, the numbers are fabricated.

## THE AXE IDENTIFIER

Every business for sale has an "axe" — the real, underlying reason the owner is selling.
Sometimes the axe is benign (genuine retirement). Sometimes it's lethal (anchor client gave
60-day notice). Your job is to find it before you sign the LOI.

### The Three-Conversation Test

Ask the same core question in three different ways across three separate interactions. If the
answer is consistent, it's probably true. If it shifts, something is being hidden.

**Conversation 1 (Initial call):** "What made you decide to sell?"

**Conversation 2 (Facility visit):** "If you were keeping the business, what would you do
differently in the next 12 months?"

**Conversation 3 (Due diligence phase):** "What's the biggest challenge the next owner will face?"

Map the answers:
- **Consistent across all three** = Likely genuine. The retiree who says "I'm tired" in every
  conversation is probably tired.
- **Story shifts or contradicts** = There's a hidden reason. The seller who says "retirement"
  on the first call but "the market is getting competitive" on the facility visit is telling
  you the business is under pressure.

### Trigger Questions

These are designed to get the seller to accidentally reveal the real problem.

| Question | What It Reveals |
|---|---|
| "What would you do differently if you were keeping the business?" | Reveals unaddressed problems the seller didn't fix because they're leaving |
| "What's the biggest challenge the next owner will face?" | Sellers sometimes answer this honestly because it feels like helpful advice, not a disclosure |
| "Why now? What changed in the last 6-12 months?" | The timing question isolates the trigger event. Genuine retirees have planned for years. Escape artists had something happen recently. |
| "If we offered you a 3-year earnout, would that be interesting?" | Escape artists recoil — they want all cash now because they know the business is declining. Genuine sellers are often open to it. |
| "Would you be comfortable introducing us to your top 3 clients?" | Escape artists will say "not until after close" — because the clients may tell you the truth |
| "Has anything changed with your key accounts in the last 6 months?" | Direct probe for the most common axe: client loss |

### The Over-Explanation Rule

**Genuine retirees don't need to convince you why they're selling.** They say: "I'm 64, I've
been doing this for 22 years, and I want to play golf." Done. No further explanation needed.

If the seller spends 10 minutes explaining why they're selling — "the business is great, I
just want to focus on other things, plus my wife wants to travel, and I've always wanted to try
consulting, and the timing is just right" — they're covering. The more elaborate the reason,
the less likely it's the whole truth.

### Common Hidden Axe Reasons

Listed from most common to least, based on deals in the SE US service business market.

| Hidden Reason | How It Leaks | How to Verify |
|---|---|---|
| **Anchor client gave notice or is reducing scope** | Revenue trend shows recent dip; seller is vague about "a few changes in the client mix" | Request A/R aging and client-level revenue for last 24 months. If a top client drops off or declines, you've found it. |
| **Lease renewal denied or rent is doubling** | Seller emphasizes "great location" but doesn't mention the lease; lease terms are not in the CIM | Request the full lease agreement. If it expires within 24 months and there's no renewal option at favorable terms, the location is a ticking clock. |
| **Health scare** | Rushed timeline, emotional when asked about the future, spouse is driving the sale process | Not always verifiable. Watch for spouse doing all communication or seller mentioning "spending more time with family." |
| **Partner or spouse forcing sale** | Inconsistency between what seller says and what spouse/partner says; legal language in CIM about "dissolution" | Ask directly: "Are all ownership parties aligned on the sale?" Discomfort = no. |
| **Regulatory action pending** | Seller avoids discussing compliance; no environmental or health inspection records provided | For car wash: check state environmental records. For janitorial: check OSHA records and business license status. For all: check county court records for pending actions. |
| **Employee exodus / key person leaving** | Seller doesn't want you talking to employees; turnover data is missing from CIM | Request payroll records for last 24 months. Compare employee names. If half the team turned over in the last 6 months, the ship is leaking. |
| **Market saturation / new competitor** | Seller talks about "the market evolving" or "new players"; revenue growth has stalled | Drive the service area. For car wash: is a new tunnel wash being built nearby? For laundromat: did a new laundromat open within 2 miles? For janitorial: are competitors undercutting on price? |

## BROKER BEHAVIOR READS

Brokers are not your adversary, but they are the seller's agent. Their behavior tells you
things the seller won't say directly.

| Broker Behavior | What It Signals | Your Response |
|---|---|---|
| Pushes for a quick LOI ("other buyers are circling") | Deal has been on market too long, or seller is desperate. This is a pressure tactic. | Ignore the pressure. Respond: "We move at the pace our due diligence requires. If another buyer wants to make an offer, that's their prerogative." |
| Slow to share financials (> 1 week after NDA) | Financials are weak, being doctored, or the seller is unresponsive (bad sign either way). | Set a deadline: "We need financials by Friday or we'll prioritize other opportunities." |
| Emphasizes "potential" and "upside" over current performance | Performance is mediocre and the price is based on a dream, not reality. | Ask: "Can we focus on trailing twelve-month actuals? We value on performance, not potential." |
| Mentions "multiple interested parties" | Standard pressure tactic. In 90% of cases, there are zero real competing offers. | Ignore completely. If it's true, your offer stands on its own merit. If it's false, calling the bluff strengthens your position. |
| Is transparent, organized, and responsive | Either a genuinely good deal with a professional broker, or a very skilled broker masking a mediocre deal. | Do not lower your guard. Apply the same rigor. The best brokers make bad deals look good. |
| Broker proactively discloses a weakness ("I should mention...") | Likely being honest — disclosing is a legal protection strategy AND a sign of integrity. | Good sign, but verify anyway. The disclosed weakness may be a distraction from a bigger hidden problem. |
| Broker badmouths the seller or seems frustrated | Seller is difficult, unrealistic on price, or the listing is going nowhere. | Opportunity for you — the broker may be an ally in getting the seller to a realistic price. Build rapport with the broker. |

## COMMUNICATION TIMING ANALYSIS

How fast people respond tells you how motivated they are. Track response times from your
first inquiry through the LOI phase.

| Response Pattern | Interpretation | Your Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| Same-day response to initial inquiry | Motivated seller and/or proactive broker. Deal is active. | Engage quickly — this seller is ready to move and may attract other buyers. |
| 2-3 day response to initial inquiry | Normal. Broker is vetting you or seller needs to approve sharing info. | Standard engagement. No urgency adjustment needed. |
| 1+ week response to initial inquiry | Not serious, or overwhelmed. Possibly a fisher. | Low-priority this deal. Follow up once, then deprioritize. |
| Response time accelerates after hard questions | Seller is engaged, wants to address your concerns, and is trying to close. | Good sign. The seller respects your diligence and wants to get to a deal. |
| Response time slows after hard questions | Seller is reconsidering, consulting their lawyer, or deciding how to spin the answer. | Patience. But if the delay extends beyond 1 week with no update, the answer is probably bad. Follow up once firmly: "We need a response by [date] to maintain our timeline." |
| Immediate response to your offer (same day) | Your offer is close to acceptable, or seller is desperate. | If fast acceptance: you may have offered too much. If fast counter: seller is engaged and realistic. |
| Silence after your offer (1+ week) | Offer is too low, or seller is shopping it to create competition. | Follow up once at 5 business days: "Checking in on our offer — we're continuing to evaluate other opportunities and would appreciate a response by [date]." |
| Seller responds at odd hours (nights, weekends) | Highly motivated. This sale is occupying their mental bandwidth. | Leverage the motivation, but be ethical. A motivated seller is a good counterparty, not a victim. |
