# The Seller's Playbook — What They Are Coached to Do

This file distills how sellers and their brokers are trained to present, package, and pitch
janitorial and commercial cleaning businesses for maximum sale price. Every section represents
something a sophisticated buyer should expect, decode, and probe.

---

## HOW BROKERS PACKAGE CLEANING BUSINESSES FOR SALE

### The Listing Title
Brokers are trained to lead with the most impressive number, not the asking price.
- "**$1.3M Revenue** Commercial Cleaning Company" — leading with revenue hides a thin margin
- "**23-Year-Old** Established Cleaning Business" — leading with age hides stagnant revenue
- "**Turnkey Absentee** Cleaning Business" — "turnkey" rarely means what buyers think
- "**Recurring Revenue**" — used even when contracts are month-to-month verbal agreements

**Decode:** Whatever they put first in the title is what they're most proud of. What they
put last (or omit) is what they're hoping you won't ask about.

### The Description Frame
Sellers are coached to use specific emotional language:
- "Rare opportunity" — usually means it sat on the market for 6+ months
- "Owner is retiring" — legitimate ~60% of the time; the other 40% masks deterioration, health
  issues, burnout, or a client that's about to leave
- "Motivated seller" — price reduction incoming or deal previously fell through
- "Turnkey operation" — almost always requires more owner involvement than stated
- "Strong recurring revenue" — verify: are these written contracts or verbal relationships?
- "Ready for new owner to take to the next level" — translation: growth has stalled and the
  seller has given up trying
- "Priced to sell" — the seller already tried a higher price and failed

### SDE Add-Back Manipulation
Sellers are required to disclose SDE (Seller's Discretionary Earnings), but they have enormous
latitude in what they add back to arrive at the SDE figure. Common legitimate add-backs:
- Owner's salary
- Owner's personal health insurance
- Personal vehicle expenses
- Non-recurring one-time expenses

**Aggressive/questionable add-backs frequently used in cleaning businesses:**
- Family member salaries (spouse on payroll doing no real work)
- Personal cell phone, travel, meals
- Equipment that was "owner's personal" but used by the business
- One-time "extraordinary" expenses that seem to recur every year
- Depreciation on equipment that genuinely needs replacing soon
- Owner draws classified as loans

**Red flag:** If you are not given a detailed add-back schedule broken down line by line,
the SDE number is unverified and should be treated as aspirational.

## THE SEVEN THINGS SELLERS HIDE (BY FREQUENCY)

### 1. Customer Concentration
The #1 hidden risk in cleaning businesses. One or two anchor clients often represent
30–60% of revenue. Sellers are coached to discuss "diversified client base" in the aggregate
while avoiding specifics. They are legally permitted to withhold client names until after
NDA is signed and sometimes until LOI is executed.

**Signs of high concentration:**
- Revenue round numbers that don't reflect organic growth
- "We serve over 30 clients" with no revenue-per-client breakdown
- A sudden spike in revenue 2–3 years ago that has since plateaued
- Broker deflects when you ask about "top 5 clients as % of revenue"

**What you need:** Top 10 clients by revenue, contract type, contract expiration, and
years of relationship. Demand this in due diligence. If any client represents >20% of
revenue, the risk of their departure is existential.

### 2. Owner-Dependent Relationships
The owner of a cleaning business is often the primary relationship manager with all major
clients. The business exists because *this person* picked up the phone every time something
went wrong at 2 AM. No amount of documentation transfers that relationship.

**Signs the business is owner-dependent:**
- Owner has been in the business since founding (common in 20+ year companies)
- The listing says "owner works X hours per week" without specifying what those hours are
- No mention of a named GM or operations manager
- The seller's transition period offer is only 2–4 weeks
- Clients are in the owner's personal network (country club, church, neighborhood)

**What you need:** Ask the broker directly: "If the current owner was removed from all
client contact on Day 1 of the transition, which clients would be at risk of leaving?" A
good seller will have an honest answer. A coached seller will deflect.

### 3. Equipment Condition and Deferred Maintenance
Cleaning businesses run on equipment: floor scrubbers, extractors, pressure washers, vans.
Sellers are coached to list the *replacement value* of FF&E, not the actual current value.
A van that cost $45,000 new and has 180,000 miles on it is not worth $45,000 — it is a
liability.

**Signs of deferred maintenance:**
- FF&E listed as a single dollar figure without itemization
- Equipment list shows "various" or approximate ages
- Business is home-based with no facility (nowhere to store/maintain equipment)
- Gross margin has been declining despite stable revenue (maintenance being cut)

**What you need:** Itemized equipment list with year purchased, current mileage/hours,
and last maintenance date. For vehicles, request maintenance logs. Budget 5–10% of revenue
annually for proper equipment maintenance in cleaning businesses.

### 4. Employee Turnover and Workforce Instability
The janitorial industry has among the highest employee turnover rates of any service sector
— often 100–200% annually in poorly managed operations. Sellers will tell you the business
has "good employees" and "strong team." This is always true on the day of the sale.

**What they won't tell you:**
- The turnover rate (always ask for the number of W-2s issued in the last 12 months vs.
  current headcount — a meaningful gap reveals true turnover)
- Whether key crew leads are loyal to the *owner* rather than the business
- Whether any employees know the business is for sale (this triggers departures)
- Whether the workforce is properly classified (misclassified 1099s = legal liability)

**What you need:** Last two years of W-2 counts, current headcount, and a breakdown of
employee tenure. Any 1099 subcontractors should be reviewed for misclassification risk.

### 5. The "Real" Reason for Selling
Sellers are coached to say "retirement," "health," or "pursuing other ventures." These are
sometimes true. But cleaning business owners also sell because:
- A major client has signaled they are leaving (and the sale closes before that happens)
- Revenue has peaked and the owner knows the next 2–3 years will be flat or declining
- A new competitor has entered the market (franchise expansion, large regional player)
- The owner is burned out and quality has been quietly declining
- A key employee (often a family member) is leaving the business
- The business has outstanding legal disputes not yet filed

**Decode signals:**
- Listings that emphasize "opportunity to grow" in a mature market = current owner can't grow it
- Sudden listing after 20+ years with no stated health or retirement trigger = watch carefully
- Seller pushes for very fast close = something is coming they don't want you to see
- Seller refuses to allow client meetings pre-close = afraid clients will say something damaging

### 6. Contract Quality vs. Contract Illusion
"Recurring revenue" is the most overused and abused term in cleaning business sales. Sellers
are trained to emphasize the word "recurring" even when:
- Contracts are month-to-month verbal agreements
- Written contracts have no minimum term and 30-day termination clauses
- "Annual contracts" auto-renew but have never been tested against a termination request

**What good contracts look like:** Multi-year (2–5 year) written agreements with creditworthy
commercial entities (hospitals, schools, government), with automatic renewal and price
escalation clauses. These command premium multiples and deserve them.

**What bad contracts look like:** Monthly agreements with small businesses or individual
property managers who chose the current company because the owner is their friend.

### 7. Margin Erosion and the Last-Mile Revenue Inflation
Some sellers inflate revenue in the 12–24 months before listing by:
- Taking on lower-margin or break-even work to boost topline
- Pulling forward revenue (billing clients for future periods early)
- Adding family members as "clients" for interim services
- Underinvesting in expenses that create a lag effect (employees departing, equipment failing)

**Signs of trailing revenue inflation:**
- Revenue has grown but SDE has not grown proportionally (margin compression)
- The trailing 12 months (T12) look better than prior years (ask for 3 years of P&Ls)
- High revenue with relatively few employees (understaffed, quality declining)
- Unusually high accounts receivable relative to revenue

## THE BROKER'S STANDARD DEFENSES

When you ask tough questions, brokers have standard responses. Here is how to read them:

| Broker says... | What it usually means |
|---|---|
| "All questions will be answered during due diligence" | They don't have the answer now and are hoping you'll be committed enough to overlook it |
| "The owner prefers not to disclose clients before LOI" | Concentration risk, or a key client is about to leave |
| "The business is priced to reflect future growth" | Current performance doesn't support the price |
| "There are multiple other interested parties" | There may be, or there may not be — it's always said to create urgency |
| "The owner is very selective about buyers" | The deal fell through previously and they are framing it as exclusivity |
| "We can't share the add-back schedule until NDA" | The add-backs are aggressive and they don't want you to walk before signing |
| "The owner would be open to a small seller note" | Full SBA financing was rejected or the price is too high for lender support |

## THE SELLER'S TRANSITION TRAP

Sellers know that a longer transition period signals more owner-dependency. So they offer
short transitions (2–4 weeks) and frame it as confidence in the business. The actual purpose
is to minimize the time during which you can discover problems with client relationships
before the deal closes.

**What a real transition looks like:** 3–6 months of post-close involvement, including
joint client meetings, crew introductions, and a performance-based earnout tied to client
retention. If a seller won't agree to any earnout, ask yourself why.

## SELLER FINANCING AS A SIGNAL

Sellers who offer meaningful seller financing (15–30% of purchase price, not token amounts
of $50K–$100K) are telling you something important: they believe the business will continue
to generate enough cash flow to pay them back. This is the most honest signal available.

Sellers who refuse all seller financing — especially in retirement sales where they
presumably don't need the money immediately — are either:
a) Expecting the business to underperform post-sale
b) Have been coached that the business can get full SBA financing (which means a lender
   validated the deal, a positive signal)
c) Have experienced a previous sale fall apart under a seller-financed structure

Ask the broker why seller financing is or isn't available. The answer is revealing.

## CAR WASH SELLER TACTICS

These are specific to car wash acquisitions and supplement the general tactics above.

### Revenue Presentation Games
- **Peak-month annualization:** Presenting the best month (typically spring/summer) × 12 instead
  of actual annual figures. Revenue can swing 30–50% by season in the SE US.
- **"Revenue per bay" or "cars per day" without context:** These numbers are meaningless without
  seasonal averages and weather normalization. Always demand 36 months of monthly revenue data.
- **Showing gross wash revenue without chemical/water costs:** Express tunnels have 25–40% COGS
  that should be netted out before comparing margins.

### Membership Inflation
- **Counting inactive members:** Sellers count everyone who ever signed up, not active paying
  members. Ask for: active members (billed in last 30 days), monthly churn rate, and average
  membership tenure.
- **"The membership program is just getting started":** This means current revenue doesn't support
  the asking multiple — they are selling you future potential, not present performance.
- **Bundling membership signup bonuses into revenue:** One-time signup fees inflating T12 revenue.

### Equipment & Real Estate Tricks
- **Presenting equipment at replacement value:** A tunnel system that cost $1.5M new but is 12
  years old is not worth $1.5M. Demand equipment age, maintenance logs, and a professional
  inspection.
- **Bundling real estate value into the business asking price:** A $600K car wash business on
  $1.2M real estate listed at $1.8M looks like a 3x SDE multiple — but the business alone may
  only justify 2x. Always separate business value from real estate value.
- **Hiding deferred maintenance on tunnels/bays:** Tunnel equipment replacement costs $500K–$2M.
  A "working" tunnel is not the same as a tunnel with 10+ years of useful life remaining.

### Weather and Location Obfuscation
- **Understating weather impact:** Rainy weeks in the SE can drop express wash revenue 30–50%.
  Hurricanes shut operations entirely. Demand monthly revenue data to see the actual seasonality.
- **Traffic count claims without verification:** "50,000 cars per day pass this location." Verify
  with state DOT data, not the seller's estimate.

## LAUNDROMAT SELLER TACTICS

These are specific to laundromat/coin laundry acquisitions.

### Revenue Verification Games
- **Coin revenue that cannot be independently verified:** This is the #1 due diligence challenge
  in laundromat acquisitions. Coin-operated machines produce no transaction log. The seller can
  claim any revenue figure and you cannot independently verify it from the machines alone.
- **Cross-verification method:** Demand utility bills (water + gas/electric) for 36 months. Water
  usage correlates directly with wash volume. If claimed revenue implies 8 turns/day but water
  bills suggest 4 turns/day, the numbers don't add up.
- **"Per turn" revenue assumptions at 100% utilization:** A machine doing 4–6 turns/day is normal.
  8+ turns/day is exceptional and should be verified. 10+ turns/day is almost certainly inflated.
- **Mixing WDF (wash-dry-fold) service revenue into self-service numbers:** WDF commands higher
  prices but requires labor. Sellers mix them together to inflate per-machine revenue metrics.

### Equipment and Facility Tricks
- **"All the machines were replaced recently":** Check serial numbers and manufacture dates, not
  seller claims. Commercial washers last 10–15 years; some sellers claim recent replacement on
  8-year-old machines.
- **Presenting machine count without age breakdown:** "42 washers and 40 dryers" tells you
  nothing. You need: brand, model, year, coin/card compatible, last service date for every unit.
- **Hiding maintenance costs behind "the machines are all working":** Working today ≠ 5+ years of
  remaining useful life. A machine that works but needs $2K in parts next year is a liability.

### Lease and Location Hiding
- **Lease renewal uncertainty:** The lease is the single most important factor in laundromat
  valuation. If the lease has < 5 years remaining with no renewal option, the business may be
  worthless by the time you pay off your loan. Sellers will avoid mentioning short lease terms.
- **Hiding upcoming rent increases:** "Rent is $4,500/month" may become $6,500/month at next
  renewal. Demand the full lease with all escalation clauses, renewal terms, and landlord
  contact information.
- **Declining neighborhood demographics:** If the surrounding area is gentrifying (renter %
  dropping as condos replace apartments), the customer base is shrinking. Sellers won't
  volunteer this trend.

### Utility Cost Hiding
- **Presenting utility costs from a cooler or low-rate period:** Dryer costs spike in humid SE
  climates. Gas rates fluctuate. Demand 36 months of utility bills, not a single month's figure.
- **Underreporting utility costs as % of revenue:** Healthy laundromats run 20–30% utility costs.
  Above 35% is a concern. If the seller claims 18%, verify with actual bills.
