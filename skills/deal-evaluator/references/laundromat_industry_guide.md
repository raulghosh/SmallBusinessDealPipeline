## 1. BUSINESS MODELS

### Unattended Self-Service (Coin/Card Operated)

The core laundromat model. Customers operate machines themselves. No staff on-site
during operating hours (or limited attendant hours for cleaning/maintenance).

| Factor | Detail |
|---|---|
| Typical revenue range | $150K–$800K/year for a single location |
| Staffing requirement | 0–1 part-time attendant for cleaning/restocking |
| SDE margin | 25–40% of gross revenue |
| Absentee suitability | **Best model for absentee ownership** |
| H1B buyer suitability | **Excellent** — minimal operational involvement required |

**Pros for absentee:**
- Lowest labor cost of any model (often $0 in true unattended)
- Revenue is automatic once machines are running
- Card/app systems enable full remote monitoring
- Predictable, recession-resistant demand

**Cons for absentee:**
- Revenue verification is harder with coin-only operations
- Vandalism and security risk without on-site staff
- Maintenance response time is critical — machines down = lost revenue
- Lower revenue ceiling than attended/WDF models

### Attended Self-Service

Same self-service machines but with a paid attendant on-site during operating hours.
Attendant handles customer service, machine issues, cleaning, and security.

| Factor | Detail |
|---|---|
| Typical revenue range | $200K–$1.2M/year |
| Staffing requirement | 1–3 attendants covering open hours |
| SDE margin | 20–35% of gross revenue (labor eats into margin) |
| Absentee suitability | **Good** — requires managing 1–3 employees |
| H1B buyer suitability | Good if attendants are self-managing; need a lead attendant |

**Pros for absentee:**
- Cleaner facility = better customer retention
- Faster maintenance response
- Enables upsell of ancillary services (detergent, snacks, WDF)
- Lower vandalism and theft

**Cons for absentee:**
- Labor cost of $25K–$60K/year depending on hours and coverage
- Employee management overhead (scheduling, turnover, payroll)
- Attendant reliability is a single point of failure for absentee owners

### Wash-Dry-Fold (WDF) Service

Drop-off service where customers leave laundry and pick it up cleaned, dried, and
folded. Charged by the pound, typically $1.50–$2.50/lb in SE US markets.

| Factor | Detail |
|---|---|
| Typical revenue range | $50K–$400K/year as add-on to self-service |
| Staffing requirement | 1–3 dedicated WDF workers during operating hours |
| SDE margin | 15–25% (labor-intensive) |
| Absentee suitability | **Moderate** — requires a reliable on-site manager |
| H1B buyer suitability | Moderate — needs a manager/lead you trust |

**Pros:**
- Higher revenue per square foot than self-service alone
- Builds customer loyalty and repeat business
- Creates revenue diversification
- Differentiates from pure self-service competitors

**Cons:**
- Labor-intensive — typically $1.00–$1.50/lb goes to labor
- Quality control issues (damage claims, lost items)
- Requires inventory management (detergent, hangers, bags)
- Manager dependency creates absentee risk

### Pickup & Delivery

Extension of WDF with vehicle-based pickup and delivery. Growing segment driven by
app-based services (Hampr, Rinse, Poplin, or proprietary).

| Factor | Detail |
|---|---|
| Typical revenue range | $30K–$200K/year as add-on |
| Staffing requirement | Driver(s) + WDF staff |
| SDE margin | 10–20% (vehicle + labor + fuel costs) |
| Absentee suitability | **Low** — logistics complexity |
| H1B buyer suitability | **Low** — too operationally intensive for passive ownership |

**Recommendation for H1B absentee buyers:** Focus on unattended or lightly attended
self-service as the primary model. WDF can be added later only if a trusted on-site
manager is in place. Avoid pickup/delivery unless a full management team exists.

## 2. EQUIPMENT

### Commercial Washers

| Type | Capacity | Price Range (New) | Expected Lifespan |
|---|---|---|---|
| Top-load coin-op (retail-grade) | 2.5–4.5 cu ft | $800–$1,200 | 5–8 years |
| Front-load commercial (small) | 18–25 lb | $3,000–$5,500 | 10–15 years |
| Front-load commercial (medium) | 30–40 lb | $5,000–$8,000 | 12–15 years |
| Front-load commercial (large) | 50–80 lb | $8,000–$12,000 | 12–15 years |
| Multi-load/express wash | 80–125 lb | $12,000–$20,000 | 12–15 years |

**Key point:** Top-load retail-grade machines in a "laundromat" are a red flag. They
indicate either under-capitalization or a very old conversion. True commercial front-load
machines from Speed Queen, Dexter, or Continental are the industry standard.

### Commercial Dryers

| Type | Capacity | Price Range (New) | Expected Lifespan |
|---|---|---|---|
| Stack dryer (30 lb x 2) | 30 lb per pocket | $4,000–$6,500 | 12–15 years |
| Single-pocket tumble dryer | 30–50 lb | $2,000–$4,500 | 10–14 years |
| Large single-pocket tumble dryer | 50–75 lb | $4,500–$8,000 | 12–15 years |
| Multi-pocket stack (2–4 pockets) | 30 lb each | $5,000–$10,000 | 12–15 years |

**Stack dryers are preferred** — they maximize revenue per square foot because they
use vertical space. A 2,000 sq ft laundromat with stack dryers can generate 30–50% more
dryer revenue than one with single-pocket units in the same footprint.

### Major Equipment Brands

| Brand | Market Position | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Speed Queen** | Industry standard, dominant in SE US | Longest track record. Parts widely available. Most service techs know them. Alliance Laundry Systems parent company. |
| **Dexter** | Premium tier | Slightly higher price point, strong reliability. Popular in newer/upgraded stores. Good tech integration. |
| **Continental/Girbau** | Value tier, growing share | Lower acquisition cost. Solid performance. Less brand recognition but competitive specs. |
| **Wascomat/Electrolux** | Legacy installs | Common in older stores. Still serviceable but parts availability declining. |
| **LG/Samsung Commercial** | New entrant | Cheaper but unproven longevity in coin-op. Limited service network. Avoid for acquisition targets. |

### Assessing Remaining Useful Life

**Critical due diligence step.** For every machine in the store, request:

1. **Brand and model number** — determines original specs and parts availability
2. **Serial number** — most manufacturers encode the build date in the serial
3. **Installation date** — when it was placed in service (may differ from build date)
4. **Maintenance records** — bearing replacements, seal changes, computer board swaps
5. **Cycle count** — modern machines track this; older ones require estimation

**Depreciation and replacement cost framework:**

| Equipment Age | Remaining Value (% of replacement) | Action |
|---|---|---|
| 0–3 years | 70–90% | Premium equipment, factor into valuation positively |
| 4–7 years | 40–65% | Mid-life, still productive, normal wear |
| 8–10 years | 20–40% | Approaching replacement window, budget for capital expenditure |
| 11–14 years | 5–20% | End of life, major repairs likely or imminent |
| 15+ years | 0–5% | Past useful life, replacement cost must be deducted from asking price |

**Rule of thumb:** If >50% of machines are 10+ years old, deduct the full estimated
replacement cost from the asking price. A full retool of a 2,000 sq ft store runs
$150K–$350K depending on machine count and brand.

### Card/Payment Systems

| System Type | Retrofit Cost | Ongoing Cost | Revenue Verification |
|---|---|---|---|
| Coin-only (existing) | $0 | Change machine maintenance | **Poor** — cash-based, unverifiable |
| Coin + card readers (retrofit) | $15,000–$40,000 | $50–$150/month software fees | **Good** — card transactions verified |
| Card/app only (full conversion) | $20,000–$50,000 | $100–$200/month | **Excellent** — 100% digital trail |
| Hybrid coin + app (PayRange) | $2,000–$8,000 (Bluetooth modules) | $0 per device (transaction fee model) | **Partial** — app transactions verified, coins are not |

**Recommendation for acquisitions:** Stores with card/app payment systems are worth a
premium because revenue is independently verifiable. If evaluating a coin-only store,
budget $15K–$40K for retrofit and use this as a negotiation lever.

## 3. REVENUE VERIFICATION

**This is the single most important due diligence area for laundromats.** Unlike
contract-based businesses, laundromat revenue is transactional and historically
cash/coin-based, making it the industry most vulnerable to inflated revenue claims.

### The Core Problem

Coin-operated laundromats generate cash. Cash can be fabricated on financial statements.
A seller can report $400K in revenue when true revenue is $280K, and there is no
receivables ledger, no client contracts, and no invoicing trail to check against.

### Verification Methods (Use All of Them)

**Method 1: Utility Bill Cross-Reference (Most Reliable for Coin Stores)**

Water usage is the strongest independent proxy for laundromat revenue. Every wash cycle
uses a measurable amount of water.

| Metric | Benchmark |
|---|---|
| Water cost as % of revenue | 4–7% for self-service stores |
| Average water per front-load cycle | 15–25 gallons (varies by machine size) |
| Average water per top-load cycle | 30–45 gallons |
| Revenue per gallon of water used | $0.08–$0.15 (varies by vend price and efficiency) |

**Process:**
1. Request 24 months of water bills (not 12 — you need to see seasonal variation)
2. Calculate total gallons used per month
3. Estimate cycles run: total gallons / average gallons per cycle
4. Multiply estimated cycles by average vend price
5. Compare to reported revenue — if reported revenue exceeds this estimate by >15%,
   demand an explanation or walk away

**Method 2: Utility Spend Ratio**

| Utility | Expected % of Revenue | Red Flag Threshold |
|---|---|---|
| Water + sewer | 4–8% | If revenue implies water % below 3%, revenue is likely inflated |
| Gas (dryers, hot water) | 6–12% | Gas unusually low vs. revenue = inflated revenue |
| Electric | 4–8% | Less reliable proxy but directionally useful |
| **Total utilities** | **20–30% of revenue** | Below 15% total utilities = either very efficient store or inflated revenue |

**Method 3: Card/App Transaction Logs**

If the store has any card or app payment system, request full transaction logs directly
from the payment processor (CSC ServiceWorks, PayRange, Dexter Pay, SpyderWash, etc.).
Do NOT accept seller-provided summaries — get raw data from the processor.

Card/app data gives you:
- Transaction count per machine per day
- Revenue per machine per day
- Peak/off-peak usage patterns
- Historical trends (growth or decline)

**Method 4: Revenue Per Square Foot Benchmark**

| Store Type | Revenue Per Sq Ft Per Year | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Self-service only, average location | $15–$20/sq ft | Bread-and-butter laundromat |
| Self-service only, strong location | $20–$30/sq ft | High-density renter area, limited competition |
| Self-service + WDF | $25–$40/sq ft | WDF adds revenue intensity |
| Premium location, full-service | $35–$55/sq ft | Urban core, high traffic, full amenities |
| Below $12/sq ft | RED FLAG | Underperforming, over-built, or bad location |

**Method 5: Revenue Per Machine Per Day**

| Machine Type | Revenue/Machine/Day (Average) | Revenue/Machine/Day (Strong) |
|---|---|---|
| Front-load washer (20–30 lb) | $15–$25 | $30–$50 |
| Front-load washer (40–60 lb) | $20–$40 | $45–$75 |
| Multi-load washer (60–80 lb) | $25–$50 | $55–$100 |
| Dryer (30 lb pocket) | $8–$15 | $18–$30 |
| Stack dryer (2 pockets) | $15–$28 | $30–$55 |

**Method 6: Turns Per Machine Per Day**

"Turns" = number of complete cycles a machine runs per day. This is the operational
heartbeat of the business.

| Performance Level | Turns/Machine/Day | What It Means |
|---|---|---|
| Underperforming | 2–3 turns | Weak location, too many machines, or declining market |
| Average | 4–6 turns | Healthy, sustainable operation |
| Strong | 7–9 turns | Good location, right machine mix, possible capacity constraint |
| Maxed out | 10+ turns | Capacity-constrained — opportunity for expansion or price increase |

## 5. LEASE DEPENDENCY

**The lease is the single most important document in a laundromat acquisition.** A
laundromat cannot be relocated — the plumbing, drainage, gas lines, and electrical
infrastructure are built into the specific space. If the lease ends or rent spikes
to unsustainable levels, the business is effectively destroyed.

### Lease Term Requirements

| Lease Factor | Minimum Acceptable | Ideal | Deal-Breaker |
|---|---|---|---|
| Remaining term | 7 years | 10+ years | Less than 5 years remaining |
| Renewal options | At least 1 five-year option | 2+ five-year options | No renewal options |
| Rent escalation | CPI-based or 2–3%/year fixed | Flat rent or 1–2%/year | >5%/year escalation or market-rate reset |
| Lease assignment clause | Assignable with landlord consent (not unreasonably withheld) | Freely assignable | Non-assignable or requires landlord approval with no standard |

### Why 7–10 Year Minimum

A laundromat retool (full equipment replacement) costs $150K–$400K+. Equipment has a
10–15 year useful life. If the lease expires in 5 years:
- You cannot recoup retool investment within the lease term
- You have zero negotiating leverage at renewal — the landlord knows you cannot move
- Equipment has no residual value if you lose the space (commercial washers are
  bolted to the floor with custom plumbing; removal and reinstallation costs are
  prohibitive)

**Rule of thumb:** Remaining lease term must exceed the payback period for any equipment
investment you plan to make. If you are buying a store needing $200K in equipment
upgrades and your payback period is 4–5 years, you need at minimum 7–8 years on the
lease to have a buffer.

### Lease Clauses to Examine

| Clause | What to Look For | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|
| **Exclusivity radius** | Landlord cannot lease to another laundromat within the same center/radius | No exclusivity = landlord could put a competitor next door |
| **Assignment/transfer** | Lease is assignable to new owner upon sale | Landlord can block the sale by refusing assignment |
| **CAM charges** | Common Area Maintenance charges — are they capped? | Uncapped CAM can escalate unpredictably |
| **Utility pass-throughs** | Who pays for water/sewer main line? Grease trap? | Surprise utility costs from landlord billing |
| **Build-out/revert clause** | If lease ends, must you restore to original condition? | Restoration costs for plumbing removal can be $20K–$50K |
| **Relocation clause** | Does landlord have right to relocate you within the center? | Forced relocation kills the investment — unacceptable for laundromats |
| **Use clause** | Does the lease restrict the permitted use? | Overly narrow use clause limits pivot options |
| **Rent abatement/free rent** | Any remaining concessions from original build-out | Indicates original build-out negotiation leverage |
| **Percentage rent** | Landlord takes % of revenue above a threshold | Unusual for laundromats but check — caps upside |

### Landlord Relationship

Before closing, have the buyer's attorney contact the landlord to:
1. Confirm lease terms match seller's representation
2. Discuss renewal intent informally
3. Assess landlord's long-term plans for the property (redevelopment risk)
4. Confirm no pending lease violations or disputes

## 6. LOCATION DEMOGRAPHICS

### What Makes a Good Laundromat Location

Laundromats serve renters who do not have in-unit laundry. The ideal location has a
high concentration of renters in apartments or older housing without washer/dryer
hookups.

### Key Demographic Benchmarks

| Metric | Target Range | Where to Find |
|---|---|---|
| **Renter percentage** | >40% within 1-mile radius (>50% is excellent) | Census Bureau ACS, ESRI Demographics |
| **Population density** | >3,000 people per sq mile | Census data, city planning |
| **Median household income** | $25,000–$60,000 | Census Bureau ACS |
| **Multi-family housing %** | >30% of housing units | Census Bureau housing data |
| **Average household size** | >2.5 people/household (more laundry per household) | Census data |

**Income sweet spot explained:** Below $25K — customers are extremely price-sensitive,
high crime risk to the store. Above $60K — most households have in-unit or in-building
laundry; demand drops sharply. The $30K–$50K band is the sweet spot where people need
laundromat services and can afford regular vend prices.

### Competition Analysis

| Factor | Benchmark |
|---|---|
| Minimum comfortable radius | 2–3 miles between laundromats in suburban areas |
| Urban minimum radius | 0.5–1 mile (higher density supports more stores) |
| Machines per capita | 1 washer per 500–800 people in the trade area is healthy |
| Red flag | 3+ laundromats within 2 miles in a suburban market |

**Competition site visits:** Before making an offer, visit every competitor within 3
miles. Assess: machine age/condition, cleanliness, pricing, payment options, parking,
hours. If competitors are newer, cleaner, and cheaper — the target store may be losing
share.

### SE US Market-Specific Notes

| State | Notes |
|---|---|
| **Florida** | Strong laundromat markets in Orlando, Tampa, Jacksonville, Miami metro areas. High renter populations. Hispanic and immigrant communities are heavy laundromat users. Hurricane risk requires insurance review. |
| **Alabama** | Lower rents, lower vend prices. Birmingham and Huntsville metros are strongest. Rural areas may lack population density. |
| **Tennessee** | Nashville metro growing rapidly — rising rents push more people into apartments without laundry. Memphis has strong demand demographics. |
| **North Carolina** | Charlotte and Raleigh-Durham metros are premium markets. High growth, increasing renter population. Fayetteville (military) is steady demand. |
| **South Carolina** | Charleston, Columbia, Greenville. Military areas (Shaw AFB, Fort Jackson) provide stable renter demand. |

### Anchor Tenant Analysis

The best laundromat locations are in strip centers with complementary anchor tenants:

| Good Anchors | Bad Anchors |
|---|---|
| Dollar stores (Dollar General, Dollar Tree) | High-end retail (Nordstrom, upscale boutiques) |
| Grocery stores (Aldi, Save-A-Lot, Food Lion) | Office parks (no foot traffic after 5 PM) |
| Check cashing / payday loan | Furniture stores (low frequency visits) |
| Hispanic grocery / ethnic food stores | Auto dealers (wrong customer base) |
| Convenience stores / gas stations | |
| Fast food restaurants | |

The ideal co-tenants share the same demographic customer base as the laundromat.

## 7. TECHNOLOGY & PAYMENT SYSTEMS

### Payment System Comparison

| System | Type | Key Features | Cost | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **CSC ServiceWorks** | Card + app | Largest network. Card and app payment. Remote monitoring. Revenue reporting. | $15K–$30K install; monthly fees | Mid-to-large stores wanting full platform |
| **PayRange** | Bluetooth app add-on | Retrofits to any coin machine. App-based payment. No card reader hardware. | $50–$100 per machine; 15% transaction fee | Cheapest retrofit for coin stores |
| **Dexter Pay** | Integrated app | Built into Dexter machines. App payment. Loyalty programs. | Included with new Dexter equipment | Stores with Dexter equipment |
| **SpyderWash** | Card + coin | Debit/credit card system. Per-machine card readers. | $200–$400 per machine; monthly fees | Adding card acceptance to existing machines |
| **Speed Queen Insights** | Integrated platform | Built into Speed Queen machines. App + card. Machine diagnostics. | Included with new SQ equipment | New/retooled Speed Queen stores |
| **CyclePay** | Card + app | Multi-brand compatible. Revenue reporting. | $15K–$25K install | Multi-brand stores wanting unified system |

### Technology Value for Absentee Owners

For an absentee owner, the payment/monitoring system is the primary management tool.
It replaces the need to physically visit the store to count coins.

**Must-have technology features for absentee operation:**
1. **Real-time revenue reporting** — see daily/weekly/monthly revenue by machine
2. **Machine status alerts** — notification when a machine goes down or errors
3. **Remote vend price adjustment** — change prices without a site visit
4. **Usage analytics** — identify peak hours, underperforming machines, trends
5. **Customer payment via app or card** — reduces cash handling and theft risk
6. **Security camera integration** — remote viewing of the store via phone/web

### Retrofit ROI Calculation

When evaluating a coin-only store, calculate the payback on a card/app retrofit:

**Typical scenario:**
- Coin-only store: $300K revenue
- Retrofit to card/app: $25K investment
- Expected revenue lift from card/app: 10–20% (customers spend more on card/app)
- Revenue increase: $30K–$60K/year
- Payback period: 6–18 months
- Added benefit: Revenue is now verifiable for future resale

**Premium at resale:** Stores with card/app systems typically sell for 0.2x–0.4x higher
SDE multiple than comparable coin-only stores, because buyers can verify revenue.

## 8. ABSENTEE VIABILITY ASSESSMENT

### Absentee Ownership Requirements (Laundromat-Specific)

For an H1B visa holder, the laundromat must operate with zero day-to-day involvement
from the owner. The owner's role is limited to: reviewing financial reports, approving
capital expenditures, and strategic decisions.

### Minimum Infrastructure for Absentee Operation

| Requirement | Priority | Monthly Cost | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Card/app payment system with remote reporting | **Critical** | $100–$200/month | This is your eyes on the business |
| Security cameras with remote access | **Critical** | $30–$60/month | Cloud-based DVR, 4–8 cameras minimum |
| Maintenance technician on call | **Critical** | $500–$2,000/month (contract) | Must respond within 4–24 hours |
| Part-time attendant (cleaning/restocking) | Recommended | $800–$1,500/month | 15–20 hrs/week for cleaning and minor issues |
| Property manager or operator (if WDF) | Required for WDF only | $2,500–$4,500/month | Full-time manager needed for WDF service |
| Bookkeeper/accountant | Required | $200–$500/month | Monthly reconciliation of card system vs. deposits |
| Change machine (if accepting coins) | Recommended | Maintenance only | Bill-to-coin changer; $3K–$6K to purchase |

### Absentee Viability Scoring (Laundromat-Specific)

| Factor | Points | Criteria |
|---|---|---|
| **Payment system** | 0–3 | 3 = card/app with full reporting; 2 = card system, partial reporting; 1 = PayRange/partial app; 0 = coin-only |
| **Equipment age** | 0–2 | 2 = avg age <5 years; 1 = avg age 5–10 years; 0 = avg age >10 years |
| **Lease security** | 0–3 | 3 = 10+ years remaining + options; 2 = 7–10 years; 1 = 5–7 years; 0 = <5 years |
| **Maintenance coverage** | 0–2 | 2 = documented maintenance contract/history; 1 = informal arrangement; 0 = owner does maintenance |
| **Revenue verifiability** | 0–3 | 3 = 100% card/app with processor records; 2 = partial card + utility cross-check; 1 = coin-only but utilities align; 0 = coin-only, no verification possible |
| **Security** | 0–1 | 1 = cameras with remote access; 0 = no cameras or local-only recording |
| **Location quality** | 0–1 | 1 = strong demographics + anchor tenants; 0 = weak or declining area |

| Score | Verdict |
|---|---|
| 12–15 | Excellent absentee candidate — suitable for H1B passive ownership |
| 9–11 | Good — minor improvements needed (e.g., add cameras, formalize maintenance) |
| 6–8 | Moderate — requires investment in systems/infrastructure pre- or post-close |
| 3–5 | Poor — significant operational gaps for absentee ownership |
| 0–2 | Not suitable — pass unless major overhaul is priced into the deal |

### Common Absentee Failure Modes

| Failure Mode | Cause | Prevention |
|---|---|---|
| Machine downtime goes undetected | No monitoring system | Card/app system with alerts |
| Vandalism / theft escalates | No cameras, no attendant | Cameras + occasional attendant presence |
| Utility costs spike unexpectedly | Leaking machines, running toilets | Maintenance contract with regular inspections |
| Lease expires without renewal | Owner did not track lease dates | Calendar the lease expiration 18 months out |
| Revenue declines gradually | New competitor opens, area demographics shift | Quarterly revenue review vs. prior year |
| Attendant theft (for attended stores) | No cash controls | Card/app system eliminates most cash handling |

## 10. VALUATION BENCHMARKS (LAUNDROMAT-SPECIFIC)

### SDE Multiples

| Multiple | What It Signals |
|---|---|
| Below 2.0x SDE | Distressed — old equipment, short lease, coin-only, declining area |
| 2.0x–2.5x SDE | Below market — equipment aging, some risk factors, or motivated seller |
| **2.5x–3.5x SDE** | **Fair market — typical for a well-maintained self-service laundromat** |
| **3.5x–4.5x SDE** | **Premium — newer equipment, card/app system, long lease, strong location** |
| 4.5x–5.5x SDE | Rich — justified only by recent retool, 10+ year lease, verified growing revenue |
| Above 5.5x SDE | Overpriced — rare to justify unless includes real estate |

**Note:** Laundromats with real estate included typically sell at 5x–8x SDE or based
on cap rate (6–9% cap rate for laundromat properties in SE US).

### Revenue Multiples

| Multiple | Context |
|---|---|
| 0.5x–0.8x Revenue | Older store, coin-only, aging equipment |
| **0.8x–1.2x Revenue** | **Typical for well-maintained store with decent equipment** |
| 1.2x–1.8x Revenue | Premium — newer equipment, strong lease, card/app system |
| Above 1.8x Revenue | Usually includes real estate or is overpriced |

### Quick Valuation Sanity Checks

| Check | Formula | Healthy Range |
|---|---|---|
| Price to monthly revenue | Asking price / monthly revenue | 24–48 months (2–4 years of revenue) |
| Cap rate | Net Operating Income / Asking Price | 15–25% for business only; 6–9% if includes real estate |
| Price per machine | Asking price / total machine count | $3,000–$8,000 per machine (business + equipment value) |
| Replacement cost comparison | Compare asking price to cost of building new from scratch | Asking price should be less than 60–80% of full build-out cost |

## 11. RED FLAGS AND DEAL-KILLERS

### Automatic Deal-Killers

These should result in an immediate pass unless the price reflects extreme distress:

1. **Lease has <5 years remaining with no renewal options** — you cannot recoup any
   equipment investment and have zero leverage at renewal
2. **Coin-only store where utility bills do not support reported revenue** — revenue
   is fabricated; walk away
3. **Seller refuses to provide utility bills or card system data** — hiding something
4. **Equipment average age >12 years with no retool plan priced in** — you are buying
   a $150K–$400K capital expenditure obligation
5. **Landlord will not consent to lease assignment** — you literally cannot buy the
   business
6. **Environmental issues** — laundromats use chemicals and have complex plumbing;
   unresolved environmental violations are extremely expensive
7. **Location losing population or renter percentage declining** — secular demand
   decline that cannot be fixed by the operator

### Serious Red Flags (Investigate Further)

1. Revenue has declined in any of the last 3 years — demand an explanation
2. Utility costs are abnormally low relative to reported revenue
3. Seller is not willing to offer any seller financing — may lack confidence
4. Machines are a mix of unrelated brands/ages — suggests piecemeal, reactive maintenance
5. No security cameras installed
6. Store hours are limited (not 24/7 or extended hours) — may indicate security problems
7. WDF revenue is >40% of total revenue — high labor dependency
8. Rent is >25% of gross revenue
9. Multiple machines out of service during site visit
10. Parking lot in poor condition or limited parking — directly impacts customer traffic

### Moderate Concerns (Price Adjustments, Not Deal-Killers)

1. Coin-only operation — budget $15K–$40K for card/app retrofit
2. Equipment age 8–12 years — budget for partial retool within 3–5 years
3. Lease term 5–7 years — negotiate extension before close or price in risk
4. No formal maintenance contract — add $6K–$24K/year to cost structure
5. Attendant-dependent operation — price in labor cost if currently owner-attended

### Insurance Requirements

| Coverage | Typical Annual Cost | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| General liability ($1M/$2M) | $1,500–$3,500 | Required by most leases |
| Property/equipment coverage | $1,000–$3,000 | Covers machine damage, fire, water damage |

### Water and Sewer Considerations

- SE US water rates are generally favorable but sewer rates vary widely by municipality
- Some municipalities charge sewer based on metered water usage — this penalizes
  laundromats because water goes down the drain (no irrigation credit)
- Check if the store has a separate sewer meter or sewer exemption — this can save
  $2K–$8K/year in sewer charges
- Grease trap requirements vary by municipality — failure to maintain can result in
  fines and forced closure
- Verify the store's plumbing capacity (water main size, drain capacity) supports the
  current machine count

## QUICK REFERENCE: DEAL EVALUATION CHECKLIST

Use this checklist when first reviewing a laundromat listing:

- [ ] Lease term remaining: minimum 7 years, ideally 10+
- [ ] Lease is assignable to new buyer
- [ ] Rent is <25% of reported gross revenue
- [ ] Equipment average age: <10 years preferred
- [ ] Equipment brand: Speed Queen, Dexter, or Continental preferred
- [ ] Payment system: card/app system present (or budget for retrofit)
- [ ] Revenue verification: utility bills align with reported revenue
- [ ] Revenue per square foot: $15–$30/sq ft for self-service
- [ ] Renter percentage: >40% within 1-mile radius
- [ ] Median household income: $25K–$60K in trade area
- [ ] No competitor within 2 miles (suburban) or 0.5 miles (urban)
- [ ] Asking price: 2.5x–4.0x verified SDE
- [ ] Absentee viability score: 9+ (from Section 8 rubric)
- [ ] Security cameras with remote access installed
- [ ] 24 months of utility bills available for review
- [ ] 24 months of card/app transaction data available (if applicable)
- [ ] Tax returns for 3 years available
- [ ] No environmental issues or code violations
- [ ] Seller willing to provide seller financing (demonstrates confidence)
