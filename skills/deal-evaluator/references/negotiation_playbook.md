# Negotiation Playbook — Small Business Acquisitions
## For Janitorial, Car Wash, and Laundromat Deals ($250K-$3M Revenue, SE US)

---

## BATNA ANALYSIS

Every negotiation outcome is determined by who has the better alternative to this deal. Assess
both sides before making an offer.

### Buyer's BATNA

Your alternatives if this deal falls through:

| Alternative | Strength | When It's Strongest |
|---|---|---|
| Other deals currently on the market | HIGH if pipeline is full, LOW if this is the only viable deal | Buyer has 3+ qualified deals in evaluation |
| Wait for a better deal | MODERATE — depends on patience and capital cost | Buyer has no timeline pressure, capital is not expensive to hold |
| Invest the capital elsewhere | LOW for acquisition-focused buyers, HIGH for flexible investors | Buyer can achieve target returns through alternatives (real estate, index funds) |
| Build from scratch (greenfield) | LOW for janitorial (slow), VERY LOW for car wash/laundromat (capital-intensive) | Almost never a strong BATNA for these verticals |

**Rule:** Never enter a negotiation where this is your only deal. If it is, slow down your
timeline and source alternatives before making an offer. Desperation is the most expensive
emotion in acquisitions.

### Seller's BATNA

The seller's alternatives if they don't sell to you:

| Signal | What It Tells You | Seller's BATNA Strength |
|---|---|---|
| Listing has been active < 30 days | Fresh listing, seller expects market-rate offers | STRONG — they have time |
| Listing has been active 90-180 days | Market has spoken; price or deal is unattractive | WEAKENING — pressure is building |
| Listing has been active 180+ days | Stale deal, seller may be unrealistic or there's a problem | WEAK — they need a deal |
| Price has been reduced 1+ times | Seller is adjusting expectations | WEAK — they're chasing the market down |
| Seller is 60+ years old, mentions retirement | Personal timeline is driving the sale | WEAKENING OVER TIME — every month of delay costs them |
| Seller has another business or income source | Sale is optional, not survival | STRONG — they can walk |
| Seller's spouse/partner is pushing the sale | Emotional pressure to close | WEAK — but may be irrational on price |

**Key insight:** A seller's BATNA almost always weakens with time. The longer a deal sits, the
more leverage shifts to the buyer. Be patient. Time is the buyer's greatest weapon in a
non-auction process.

## ANCHORING STRATEGY

### Where to Open on Price

| Scenario | Opening Offer Range | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Fairly priced deal (asking = 2.5-3.0x verified SDE) | 15-25% below asking | Standard negotiation range; expect to land 5-15% below asking |
| Overpriced deal (asking = 3.5x+ SDE or unverified numbers) | 25-40% below asking | You need room to move AND to signal that the asking price is unrealistic |
| Underpriced deal (asking < 2.0x verified SDE) | 5-10% below asking | Don't get greedy — move fast before another buyer appears |
| Distressed deal (seller in crisis, business declining) | 40-50% below peak valuation | Price for the risk; the business may continue declining post-close |

### Anchoring Rules

1. **Always anchor with data, not feelings.** Frame your offer as: "Based on comparable
   transactions in the SE US for businesses of this size and profile, and applying a
   conservative DCF at current interest rates, we've arrived at..."

2. **Never anchor first on seller financing terms.** Let the seller propose their financing
   structure. If they offer 10% seller note at 6% over 5 years, you know their floor. Then
   counter: 15% seller note at 5% over 7 years.

3. **Anchor the total deal value, not just the price.** A $400K price with a $60K seller note
   at 5% is different from $400K all-cash. Frame your offer as a package from the start.

4. **If the broker gives you "the seller won't accept anything below X," treat X as the
   ceiling, not the floor.** Brokers telegraph the seller's real minimum frequently. Offer
   10-15% below that number.

## CONCESSION LADDER

When negotiating, concede items in this order — from most valuable to you (give last) to
least valuable (give first). The key is to trade concessions, never give them away for free.

### Concession Priority (Top = Hardest to Give, Bottom = Easiest)

| Priority | Concession | Value to Buyer | Typical Range | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Purchase Price** | HIGHEST | Negotiate 10-25% below asking | This is the lever with the largest impact on returns. Fight hardest here. |
| 2 | **Seller Financing Terms** | HIGH | 10-20% of price as seller note, 5-7% rate, 5-7 year term | Seller note = alignment. If seller won't carry any note on a retirement sale, that is a red flag. |
| 3 | **Transition Period Length** | MODERATE-HIGH | 30-90 days standard; push for 90-180 if owner is the business | Longer transition = more value to buyer, but costs seller time. Trade: "We'll agree to your price if you do a 6-month transition." |
| 4 | **Earnout Structure** | MODERATE | 10-20% of price tied to 12-24 month revenue retention | Protects buyer if customers leave. Seller should be confident enough to accept if the business is real. |
| 5 | **Non-Compete Scope** | MODERATE | 50-100 mile radius, 3-5 years, same industry | Essential for janitorial — seller knows every client personally. Less critical for car wash/laundromat. |
| 6 | **Training Period** | LOW-MODERATE | 2-4 weeks hands-on, then phone availability for 6-12 months | Often included in transition; separate it if you need a concession to trade. |
| 7 | **Equipment/Inventory Inclusion** | LOW | All equipment included at close, inventory at cost | Standard in most deals, but can be carved out if you need to reduce price. |

### How to Trade Concessions

Never concede without getting something in return. Use conditional language:

- "We can move to $380K **if** you extend the seller note to 7 years at 5%."
- "We'll accept the 90-day transition **if** the non-compete covers a 75-mile radius for 5 years."
- "We're comfortable with a 15% earnout reduction **if** you provide 6 months of post-close phone support."

**Never say:** "We can go up to $380K." This gives away your movement without locking anything in.
**Always say:** "We'd be willing to move to $380K if [condition]."

## INFORMATION ASYMMETRY TACTICS

### Questions That Reveal Seller's Position

Ask these in a conversational tone, ideally across multiple interactions — not as an interrogation.

| Question | What It Actually Tests | Red Flag Answer |
|---|---|---|
| "How long have you been considering selling?" | Urgency and planning horizon | "A few weeks" (reactive, something just happened) or "years" (can't find a buyer) |
| "Have you had other offers?" | Competition and market validation | "Several" with no specifics (likely bluffing) or "No" (validates your leverage) |
| "What would the ideal transition look like for you?" | Seller's priorities beyond price | "As fast as possible" (wants out — why?) |
| "If the right deal doesn't come along, what's your plan?" | BATNA strength | "I'll just keep running it" = strong BATNA. "I don't know" = weak BATNA. |
| "What's your biggest concern about selling?" | Hidden deal-breakers | Anything about employees, clients leaving, or legal exposure |
| "How did you arrive at the asking price?" | Sophistication and flexibility | "My broker told me" = flexible. "I know what it's worth" = anchored. |
| "Are there any upcoming changes I should know about — leases, contracts, employees?" | Undisclosed risks | Hesitation or vague answers = something is pending |

### Information You Never Reveal

| Never Share | Why |
|---|---|
| Your maximum budget | Seller will price to your ceiling |
| Your timeline pressure (visa deadlines, loan expiration, etc.) | Seller will slow-play to extract concessions |
| That this is your first acquisition | Seller and broker will assume you'll overpay |
| How excited you are about the deal | Enthusiasm = willingness to overpay in seller's mind |
| That you've already been approved for SBA financing | Removes financing contingency as leverage |

## DEAL STRUCTURE LEVERS

### Financing Mix

| Structure | When to Use | Buyer Advantage | Seller Advantage |
|---|---|---|---|
| SBA 7(a) + Seller Note (10-15%) | Default structure for most deals | Low down payment, long amortization, seller alignment | Seller gets most of price at close, deferred income on note |
| All-cash (rare at this level) | Distressed deal where speed wins | Maximum leverage on price (offer 20-30% less than financed deal) | Certainty of close, no contingencies |
| Larger Seller Note (20-30%) | Seller is flexible, wants ongoing income | Lower SBA borrowing, stronger alignment | Regular income stream, potential tax advantages |
| SBA + Earnout (no seller note) | Seller won't carry a note | Earnout protects against revenue drop | Seller avoids credit risk of note |

### Earnout Design

- **Tie to revenue, not profit.** Profit is too easy for the buyer to manipulate post-close.
- **Measurement period:** 12-24 months. Shorter = less useful. Longer = seller won't agree.
- **Typical structure:** 10-20% of purchase price, paid quarterly, based on trailing revenue
  vs. baseline (usually TTM revenue at close).
- **Include a floor and ceiling:** Seller gets minimum 50% of earnout if revenue drops < 20%.
  Buyer caps payout at 100% of earnout amount.

### Escrow

- **Hold 5-10% of purchase price in escrow for 6-12 months.**
- Purpose: covers undisclosed liabilities (tax liens, pending lawsuits, employee claims,
  unreported equipment liens).
- Release conditions: clean and clear after escrow period, or applied against verified claims.
- **Seller resistance to escrow is a yellow flag.** Legitimate sellers have nothing to fear.

### Working Capital Adjustment

- Ensure adequate working capital (cash, receivables, inventory) is included at close.
- For janitorial: 1-2 months of operating expenses in WC is standard.
- For car wash/laundromat: WC needs are lower (cash businesses), but ensure supply inventory
  (chemicals, soap, etc.) is included.
- Define WC target in the LOI and true-up at close based on closing balance sheet.

## WALKAWAY CRITERIA

Hard stops — if any of these are true, do not proceed regardless of price.

| Criterion | Rationale |
|---|---|
| **Seller refuses all financing on a retirement sale** | If the seller won't put 10-15% at risk on their own business, they know something. On non-retirement sales (partnership breakup, etc.) this is less alarming. |
| **SDE multiple > 4.0x with no multi-year written contracts** | You're paying a premium for cash flow that could evaporate next month. No contracts = no predictability = no justification for premium pricing. |
| **Customer concentration > 40% in top 2 clients** | Losing one client collapses the business. In janitorial, this is the single most common deal-killer. |
| **Franchise** | Always walk. Franchise fees, territory restrictions, mandatory vendors, and transfer approval requirements destroy deal economics and owner flexibility. |
| **Absentee score < 4/10 with no clear path to improvement** | If the business requires an owner-operator and you're buying for passive income, no amount of transition planning will fix a fundamentally owner-dependent business. |
| **Lease expires within 2 years with no renewal option** | For car wash and laundromat, the lease IS the business. Losing it means total loss. For janitorial (if location-dependent), same logic applies. |
| **Seller won't provide tax returns** | Non-negotiable. If the seller won't show tax returns, the claimed financials are fiction. |
| **Material litigation pending or recent regulatory action** | Unless the litigation is fully indemnified by seller in the purchase agreement, the risk is unquantifiable. Walk. |

### Soft Walkaway Signals (Not Automatic, But Require Answers)

| Signal | Action Required |
|---|---|
| Employee turnover > 30% annually | Demand explanation and verify. High turnover in janitorial is common but manageable. In car wash/laundromat, it signals management problems. |
| Revenue declining > 10% YoY | Understand why. Lost a client? Market shift? Owner checked out? Price must reflect the decline trajectory, not the peak. |
| Broker is evasive on basic questions | Escalate directly to the seller. If both are evasive, walk. |
| Equipment is at end of useful life | Not a walkaway — but price must reflect the imminent CapEx. Deduct full replacement cost from your offer. |
